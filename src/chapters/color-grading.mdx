The color panel in a raw editor is a stack of controls run in one order, and the first move in that stack is different in kind from every move after it. White balance is a correction. There is a right answer, the neutral, and you can find it by making something that should be grey read as grey. Everything after white balance is a choice: the profile you start from, the single hue you deepen, the color you pour into the shadows. There is no correct version of any of those, only a chosen one. Beginners blur the two, nudging temperature for mood and dropping teal into the shadows to fix a cast, and the result goes muddy because a fix and a taste are fighting over the same pixels. Keep them separate and in order, correct first and choose second, and the rack of color sliders stops being a mood board and becomes a short pipeline you can reason about. This is [order of operations](/order-of-operations) applied to color, it rides entirely on the [raw file](/the-digital-negative), and it assumes the way of seeing color from [an earlier chapter](/color-science-for-photographers): that color is a relationship, and every hue has a true opposite across the wheel.

## The panel is a stack, and one stage is a correction

Read the color controls from the top of the develop module down and they form a pipeline from the most global decision to the most targeted. First the profile, the baseline rendering that turns raw sensor data into color at all. Then white balance, which sets the neutral. Then the color mixer, the old HSL panel, which retargets one band of hue at a time. Then the color grading wheels, which push a chosen color into the shadows, midtones, and highlights for mood. The order is not arbitrary: each stage assumes the one above it is settled, which is why white balance, sitting near the top, has to be right before any of the lower choices mean anything. Grade a frame that is still too warm and you are stacking a chosen warmth on top of an accidental one, with no way to tell them apart later.

## White balance is the one correction

White balance answers a single question: what was the color of the light, so it can be discounted and neutrals rendered neutral. It runs on two axes. Temperature is the blue to amber axis, measured in kelvin, and the scale runs backwards from intuition. It comes from blackbody radiation, where a physically hotter radiator glows bluer, so warm looking light sits at low numbers and cool looking light at high ones. Cambridge in Colour lists the ranges: candlelight around 1000 to 2000 K, tungsten bulbs 2500 to 3500 K, midday daylight 5000 to 6500 K, overcast 6500 to 8000 K, and open shade climbing toward 9000 to 10000 K. About 5000 K is roughly neutral. The second axis is tint, the green to magenta shift, which the daylight cases barely need but which fluorescent and mixed artificial light demand, because those sources push color green or magenta in a way no temperature setting can undo. In Adobe's editors the Temp slider warms the picture as you drag it right and cools it as you drag it left, and Tint adds magenta to the right and green to the left.

The reason there is a right answer rather than a preference is that you can measure it. Photograph a grey card under the light, or click the white balance eyedropper on any spot in the frame that should be neutral, and the converter solves the temperature and tint that render that spot exactly neutral. Adobe's Camera Raw does precisely this: clicking a neutral target makes it read the color of the scene's light and set both sliders so the cast disappears. The widget below is that hunt made physical. The scene was shot under a warm cast, so it starts too orange, and there is exactly one point on the temperature and tint plane where the grey card goes truly neutral. Watch the grey card, not the whole frame, and you will find it.

The move is nearly free, and only on a raw file. White balance is not baked into a raw capture. It is stored as an instruction and applied during conversion as a set of per-channel gain multipliers, scaling the red and blue channels up or down to catch the green channel, which stays fixed as the reference. FastRawViewer puts real numbers on it: correcting for a cool 10000 K light lifts red by about a stop and a half while blue barely moves, and correcting for a warm 2500 K light lifts blue by about a stop and a half while red barely moves. Because it is only a linear rescaling of channel data the converter has not yet committed to, changing your mind costs nothing. A JPEG is the opposite. Its white balance was applied in camera into a gray-balanced space, so re-correcting a cast later spends bit depth and gamut you cannot get back. This is the practical half of a promise from [the digital negative](/the-digital-negative): the warmth of a room is yours to keep or discard in the edit, with no penalty either way, precisely because the raw file never decided.

## The profile is where color starts, and where two cameras differ

Before a single slider moves, every raw file has a profile applied. A profile is the set of instructions that renders the raw sensor data into the colors and tones you see, and Julieanne Kost is blunt that it is the foundation of the rendering: it is applied first, it is not a preset, and it does not touch your slider values. Since April 2018 the default has been Adobe Color, which replaced the older Adobe Standard, and alongside it Adobe ships a Profile Browser with Camera Matching profiles that emulate the color styles from the camera's own menus, so a raw file can be made to match the maker's JPEG rendering. Choosing the profile is the real first color decision, and it is a choice, not a correction. Adobe Color and a Camera Matching profile are both valid renderings of the same neutral scene. They simply disagree about taste.

Underneath the profile browser sits the older Calibration panel, a Shadows Tint slider plus a Hue and Saturation control for each of the red, green, and blue primaries. Where the color mixer targets only the areas of a specific hue, the Calibration sliders redefine the primaries themselves and move every pixel that contains them, which made the panel the old home for building camera-matching and creative looks by hand. Profiles have largely absorbed that job. The panel is worth knowing because it explains what a profile is doing under the hood, a global remap of how the primaries render, rather than because you will often reach for it now.

This stage is exactly where the two cameras this book is built around start from different color, by design. Hasselblad calls its system the Natural Colour Solution, a color management pipeline it developed to run from capture through to editing, aimed at genuine, true-to-life color, and it makes a specific promise about skin: it keeps subtle tones smooth between highlights and shadows and works to leave skin tones as unaffected as possible even after adjustments. It is applied when Phocus renders the 3FR raw in Hasselblad's own working space, through color matrices and the Hasselblad Film Curve, not burned into the file. Leica's Q3 renders its 14-bit DNG through Maestro processing and offers Leica Looks, named creative styles like Classic, Contemporary, and Selenium that you browse and load through the Leica FOTOS app. Neither rendering is correct and the other wrong. They are two houses' taste in color, expressed at the profile stage, which is why matching them later is a real task and not a bug.

## Then the choices: the mixer and the grading wheels

With the profile chosen and the neutral set, the rest of the color panel is deliberate. The color mixer, renamed from the HSL panel, splits the spectrum into eight bands, red, orange, yellow, green, aqua, blue, purple, and magenta, and gives each three controls. Hue shifts the band to a neighboring shade, so a blue sky can be walked from cyan toward purple. Saturation changes that band's vividness. Luminance changes its brightness, which is the lever behind the deep blue sky, since dropping blue luminance darkens the sky without touching anything else. The targeted adjustment tool makes this direct: pick it up, drag on the sky itself, and the editor moves whichever bands live under your cursor. This is surgical color, one region of the wheel at a time, and it is a different instrument from the global Saturation and Vibrance sliders up in the Basic panel. Saturation there moves every color equally. Vibrance is nonlinear, pushing the less saturated colors harder while protecting the ones already vivid, and it is deliberately biased to leave orange, red, and yellow, the skin tones, comparatively alone. That bias is why Vibrance is the portrait photographer's slider and flat Saturation is not.

The grading wheels are the last and most cinematic stage. In 2020 Adobe replaced the two-zone Split Toning panel, which could only tint shadows and highlights, with Color Grading and its three-way wheels for shadows, midtones, and highlights, plus a global wheel over the whole image. On each wheel the handle's angle sets the hue and its distance from the center sets the saturation, so the center is no color at all, and a Luminance slider under each wheel lightens or darkens that tonal range. Two more controls decide how the ranges meet: Blending, which runs 0 to 100 and defaults to 50, sets how much the three zones overlap, and Balance shifts the midpoint that separates them. The archetypal move is teal and orange, and it is pure [color science](/color-science-for-photographers). Push the shadows toward teal and the highlights toward orange. Skin sits in the orange band of the wheel, so warm highlights hold the subject, and teal is orange's complement, so a cool background falls to the opposite side of the wheel and the face separates. It works because it places subject and surround across a diameter, not because teal and orange are magic.

## Matching two color sciences

Shooting two bodies puts the whole chapter to work at once, because a set that lurches between Leica color and Hasselblad color reads as a mistake even when every frame is fine on its own. The fix is the same correct-then-choose order, applied across cameras. Shoot a neutral reference, a grey card or a color chart, with each body under the light you are using, and set each camera's white balance off that reference, so both start from the same neutral. Then bring their baseline renderings together. The strongest version builds a custom DNG camera profile from a ColorChecker chart, which X-Rite's workflow exists to do, and which the DNG Profile Editor makes concrete: open the chart's raw, set white balance on a neutral patch, match the colored patches, and save a profile that makes that camera render the chart correctly. Because even two bodies from one maker capture color a little differently, a per-camera profile is what pulls them into agreement. Only once both cameras are corrected to a shared neutral and profiled to a shared baseline do you grade, and you grade the set once, so the look lands on top of a matched foundation rather than papering over two different ones. Colorists compress this to a rule: match first, then grade.

The reason any of this is possible is the same reason white balance is free. Both cameras record raw, the Q3 to 14-bit DNG and the X2D II to 16-bit 3FR, so each maker's color science is a starting rendition and not a decision baked into the pixels. Hasselblad's Natural Colour Solution and Leica's Maestro rendering are opinions applied at conversion, and an opinion applied at conversion can be neutralized, matched, and then regraded to a look you chose. The two houses hand you different color out of the box. What you do with it is correction first, then a single grade over the top, exactly the stack this chapter has been building.

<Callout>

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Find the neutral by the grey card">
On a frame with an obvious cast, ideally one you shot with a grey card or a white wall in it, click the white balance eyedropper on the surface that should be neutral. Watch the temperature and tint jump to the values that render it grey. Now drag Temp a few hundred kelvin each way and watch the neutral break. You have felt the thing that makes white balance a correction and not a taste: there is one setting the measurement points to, and you can land on it exactly.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Correct, then choose, and keep them apart">
Take a warm indoor frame and do the two jobs as two separate steps. First neutralize it fully with the eyedropper, until the whites are genuinely white, and look at how clinical it reads. Then, as a deliberate second move, add a little warmth back with Temp for mood. Notice that you now know exactly how much warmth is a choice, because you started from a measured neutral. Compare it to eyeballing the warmth in one pass and you will see why the two-step order is worth the extra minute.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Build teal and orange on the wheels">
On a portrait with warm skin, open the Color Grading wheels. Drag the shadows wheel toward teal and the highlights wheel toward orange, both gently, then work the Balance and Blending until the face stays warm and the background cools. Then push too far, until the skin turns and the grade announces itself. Ease back to the last setting where the effect is felt but not named. You are placing the subject and its surround on opposite sides of the wheel, which is why it separates.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="04" title="Match two cameras off one grey card">
If you shoot two bodies, or two different cameras of any kind, photograph the same grey card under the same light with each. In the edit, set white balance off the card on both files, then compare a neutral scene shot on each. The residual difference you still see is the color science, the profile talking. Try a Camera Matching profile or a custom one to close the gap, and only then apply any grade. That is the order that keeps a mixed-camera set from lurching between two renderings.
</ExerciseCard>
