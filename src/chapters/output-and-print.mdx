The file you finished in the develop module is not a photograph anyone can see. It is a master: a wide, sixteen-bit description of the picture that holds more tone and more color than any single screen or sheet of paper can show. Output is the last craft in the book, the step that turns that master into a copy made for one particular place. Three things change in the copy, its size, its color space, and the sharpening that suits the medium, and a fourth change waits for anything you print, because a sheet of paper is a smaller container than a screen and the picture has to be fitted into it. [Order of operations](/order-of-operations) put this stage last on purpose. Everything before it built the master. This chapter spends it.

## The master and its copies

Nothing you do at output touches the master. Export reads the [raw negative](/the-digital-negative) and its list of edit instructions, applies them, and writes a brand new file: a JPEG for the web, a TIFF for the lab, a small copy for a client to look over. The negative and its recipe sit untouched, so you can export the same picture ten different ways and spend none of them, exactly as the [catalog](/ingest-and-catalog) makes virtual copies that cost no pixels. Output is a set of copies, not a save.

Every one of those copies is built by the same four moves, and once you can name them, every export dialog in every program is the same dialog. Resize the pixels to what the destination wants. Convert the color into the space the destination reads. Sharpen for the size and the surface it will be seen on. And for anything printed, remap the tones and colors the paper cannot hold. The first three apply to a screen and a print alike. The fourth is where paper stops being a bright screen made of ink and becomes its own smaller thing.

## Resolution has two jobs, and ppi is not dpi

A screen and a print ask for size in different currencies, and confusing the two is the most common output mistake. On a screen, a pixel is a pixel. All that matters is the pixel dimensions, so you resize to the long edge the destination wants, maybe 2048 pixels for a web upload, and the resolution field in the export box, the one that says 240 or 300 ppi, is a note the screen never reads. Pixels per inch only means something once there are inches, and a screen has none.

A print has inches, so on paper ppi becomes the ruler. The standard for a photograph held at reading distance is 300 pixels per inch, the density at which the eye can no longer pick out a single pixel with its nose against the paper. Drop to 240 ppi and most people cannot tell the difference at a normal viewing distance, and the farther a print will be seen from, the less it needs: a gallery canvas is fine near 180, a poster near 150, a billboard read from across a street lives happily around 20 to 50. Printers have a native pitch of their own that the driver resamples to, 360 ppi on Epson pro machines and 300 on Canon and HP, a separate number from the printer's advertised dpi. Dots per inch counts the tiny ink droplets the printer sprays, 1440 or 2880 of them per inch, and it takes a cluster of those few-colored dots to build the color of one full pixel. Many dots make one pixel. That is the whole of ppi versus dpi.

Put your two cameras through the ruler and the numbers are freeing. The [Leica Q3](/choosing-a-lens), at 60.3 megapixels, is 9520 by 6336 pixels, which prints about 31.7 by 21.1 inches at a full 300 ppi with no upscaling, and about 39.7 by 26.4 inches if you accept 240. The [Hasselblad X2D II 100C](/the-digital-negative), at 100 megapixels and 11656 by 8742 pixels, prints about 38.9 by 29.1 inches at 300 ppi, and near 48.6 by 36.4 at 240. Both are larger than almost any wall most photographs ever hang on, so resolution is rarely what limits your print size. When you do want bigger, upsampling can invent the extra pixels, but it invents no extra detail: a clean native file at 150 to 200 ppi will beat the same picture aggressively scaled up to hit 300.

## Output sharpening is the last pass, and it aims at the medium

Sharpening is not one step but three, a model Bruce Fraser and Jeff Schewe built and sold as the PhotoKit Sharpener plug-in, and the three passes correct three different softnesses known at three different times. Capture sharpening comes first and offsets the blur baked into every digital capture, the demosaicing of the color array and the finite sharpness of the [lens](/lenses-optics-and-perspective). Creative sharpening is the optional middle pass, applied locally to push the detail you want the eye to hold, the sharpening cousin of the [masks](/local-adjustments-and-masking) you dodge and burn with. Output sharpening is the last pass, and it belongs to the destination alone.

It has to be last, because the right amount depends on facts you do not have until the very end. Fraser's target is a sharpening halo a fixed fraction of an inch wide, between a fiftieth and a hundredth, which at 300 ppi works out to roughly three pixels but at another size works out to another number of pixels entirely. Sharpen and then resize and the resampling smears the halo you carefully set. Worse, downsizing softens on its own, because shrinking averages many pixels into each surviving one, which is exactly why a picture reduced for the web needs a fresh sharpening pass after it is small. And the surface matters as much as the size: matte and cotton papers drink the ink and let each dot spread, a softening called dot gain, so they need a heavier hand than smooth glossy stock, and a screen, with no ink to spread at all, needs the lightest touch of the three.

This is why Lightroom folds output sharpening into the export and print dialogs rather than the develop panel. You tell it the destination, choosing Sharpen For Screen, Matte Paper, or Glossy Paper, and an Amount of Low, Standard, or High, and it computes the actual sharpening from the media and the resolution you asked for, on top of whatever you did in Develop. You never see this pass at full size, and that is the point. A file sharpened correctly for a print or a small web copy can look harsh and crunchy at 100 percent on your screen, because the sharpening was tuned for the paper or the downsample, not for pixel peeping. Trust the destination, keep the pass terminal, and back off only when you can see a bright rim halo along the hard edges.

## Screen and paper are two different boxes

Here is the fact that surprises everyone who prints for the first time. A screen makes its own light and a print does not. A monitor is emissive: every white is a lit pixel throwing hundreds of candela at your eye, and its contrast is deep, roughly 1000 to one on a decent LCD and effectively unlimited on an OLED, whose black is simply a pixel switched off. A print is reflective. It has no light of its own and can only bounce back a fraction of whatever falls on the room, so its brightest possible white is the paper itself and its darkest possible black is the most ink the paper will hold. Between those two limits a glossy print reaches maybe 200 to one and a matte print only around 100 to one, a small fraction of the screen you edited on.

That darkest black has a name and a number. Dmax is the maximum density a paper and ink can reach, defined as the negative base-ten logarithm of how little light the black patch reflects, and because it is a log scale every 0.3 of density is one photographic [stop](/iso-sensor-and-dynamic-range). Matte and cotton rag papers, which scatter light off a toothy surface, top out near a Dmax of 1.5 to 1.7. Luster sits around 2.0. Glossy and baryta papers, which let the ink lie flat and reflect a deep black, reach 2.3 to 2.5 and rival a darkroom print. The gamut shrinks along with the tonal range: paper holds fewer of the bright saturated colors a screen can throw, especially in the blues and cyans and greens, so a vivid sky or a neon sign that glows on the display has nowhere to go on the sheet. A print is a smaller box than a screen in three ways at once, a lower ceiling, a higher floor, and narrower walls, and output is the work of fitting the picture inside it.

There is a quieter reason prints come back darker than the screen promised, and it is not the file. Editing monitors ship far too bright, often 200 to 400 candela per square meter, while a display set to match a print wants only 80 to 120. A screen that bright lets you lift shadows during editing that a reflective print physically cannot reproduce, so the print reads dark and flat even when it is technically correct. The fix is to turn the monitor down toward print levels, not to lighten the file, and to judge the print under a decent, roughly 5000 Kelvin light, since paper can only be as bright as the light you give it.

## Soft proofing shows the print before you make it

You do not have to guess at any of this, because a soft proof previews the smaller box on your screen. Every paper on every printer has an ICC profile, a measured description of exactly which colors that paper, ink, and machine can reproduce, and soft proofing uses it to show, on your calibrated monitor, what the print will actually look like. Turn on Simulate Paper and Ink and the preview stops using screen white and screen black and starts using the paper's real off-white and its true achievable black, which is why a good soft proof looks flatter and duller than the picture did a moment ago. That flatness is not a bug. It is the print, arriving early enough to do something about.

The one decision the proof asks you to make is the rendering intent, the rule for how the out-of-gamut colors come in. The ICC defines four, but photographers live on two. Perceptual compresses and shifts the whole gamut inward together, moving even the colors that would have fit, so that the relationships between colors survive and nothing clips hard. Relative colorimetric does the opposite: it leaves every color that already fits exactly where it was and clips only the ones outside the boundary onto the nearest color the paper can make. Perceptual is the safer choice when a lot of the picture is out of gamut, a saturated sunset that would otherwise clip in chunks. Relative is better when only a little is outside, because most of the frame prints untouched, at the risk that several out-of-gamut colors all clip to the same edge and lose their separation. There is no universal winner. You switch between them on the actual image and keep the one that looks better.

Two more controls finish the proof. Black point compensation, on by default with relative colorimetric, maps the darkest black in your file to the darkest black the paper can print, so the shadows are rescaled to fit rather than crushed into one flat blob, which is usually what you want. And a gamut warning overlays the colors that fall outside, so you can find the trouble before it prints. The working method that follows is simple. Soft proof against the paper, compare the two intents, turn on black point compensation, and then make your print corrections, brightening the shadows and adding back a little contrast and saturation to answer the compressed box, on a proof copy so the [screen master](/ingest-and-catalog) stays exactly as you left it. Then send it, and let a real test print settle the last small argument between the monitor and the paper.

## Color space: edit wide, deliver right

The last transform is the color space, and the rule is edit wide and deliver narrow. All through the develop stage you want the biggest container you can get, a wide working space like ProPhoto RGB or Adobe RGB, carried in [sixteen bits](/the-digital-negative) so the saturated colors and smooth skies have room and do not band. Delivery is where you make it small, and where you make it small depends entirely on where it is going.

For the web, deliver sRGB, every time you do not control the far end. sRGB is the space the internet assumes, the one a browser or a phone falls back to when an image arrives with no profile attached, and services like Instagram routinely strip the profile to save bytes. Send a wide-gamut file into that pipeline and its numbers get read as sRGB by mistake, so the colors come out wrong: usually dull and desaturated on an ordinary screen, and oddly garish on a wide-gamut one. Convert to sRGB first and the numbers mean what the viewer thinks they mean. Display P3, the wider space modern phones and laptops can show, is worth delivering only when you control the whole chain, a color-managed browser reading an embedded profile on a wide-gamut display, and even then you ship an sRGB version as the fallback.

Print is the exception that proves the rule, because for paper you do not narrow to sRGB at all. sRGB is smaller than many papers can print, so throwing away the wide data before the printer sees it would crop colors the paper could have made. Instead you hand the print pipeline the widest, sixteen-bit data you have and let the paper's ICC profile do the mapping you just soft-proofed. This is where the [two color sciences in this book](/color-grading) meet their last test. The warm, contrasty Leica rendering and the smooth Hasselblad Natural Colour Solution both live, in the raw, in a gamut far larger than a screen. When you post them they collapse together into one sRGB JPEG and have to survive that squeeze; when you print them you give the lab everything and let the paper decide. Same master, two destinations, and the craft is knowing which copy each one needs.

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Make three copies of one master">
Take one finished frame and export it three ways from the same file: a web JPEG at sRGB, 2048 pixels on the long edge, sharpened for Screen at Standard; a full-resolution sixteen-bit TIFF in your wide working space for a print lab; and a small proof JPEG for a phone. Open the catalog afterward and confirm the master is exactly as you left it. You have made three deliveries and spent nothing, which is the whole shape of output.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Find your camera's native print size">
Take your camera's pixel dimensions and divide each by 300, then by 240. For the Q3 that is about 31.7 by 21.1 inches at 300 ppi and 39.7 by 26.4 at 240; for the X2D II it is about 38.9 by 29.1 and 48.6 by 36.4. Measure the largest wall you would ever hang a print on and compare. For almost everyone the wall runs out long before the pixels do, which tells you that resolution is rarely the thing to worry about.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Soft proof and switch the intent">
Load a saturated frame, a sunset or a red door or a deep blue sky, and turn on soft proofing with a real paper profile, one your lab publishes or one bundled with your printer. Turn on Simulate Paper and Ink and watch it flatten. Now switch between Perceptual and Relative Colorimetric and toggle the gamut warning, and decide which intent keeps the picture you meant. Make a proof copy, lift the shadows and add a little contrast to answer the smaller box, and leave the master alone.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="04" title="Sharpen for the surface, then look at the right size">
Export the same frame twice, once sharpened for Screen at a small web size and once sharpened for Matte Paper at full print size. View the web copy at its true size on a page, not zoomed in, and the print file at 100 percent. The web file looks right when small even though it would look crunchy at full zoom, and the matte file carries a heavier bite that the paper will drink back to normal. That gap between how a file looks and how it will be seen is exactly what output sharpening is for.
</ExerciseCard>
