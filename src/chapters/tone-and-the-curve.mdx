Every tonal control in a raw editor does the same thing underneath. Exposure, contrast, highlights, shadows, whites, blacks, and the curve itself all take the brightness a pixel came in with and hand back the brightness it goes out with. Draw that relationship, input along the bottom and output up the side, and you have the tone curve. It is the one mapping the rest of the tone panel is built on, and once you can read it the rack of sliders stops being a bag of tricks and becomes a set of handles on a single shape. This chapter is about that shape and the two decisions that matter most in it: where the ends of the range fall, which is the black point, the white point, and how much detail you can pull back near them, and how steeply the tones in between are spread, which is contrast. One rule sits over all of it. A tone shoved against the wall is gone, and no curve brings it back. [The digital negative](/the-digital-negative) is the file you are shaping, and [order of operations](/order-of-operations) is why tone comes after you have set white balance and before you go local.

## The curve is a transfer function

Put input brightness on the horizontal axis, shadows on the left and highlights on the right, and output brightness on the vertical. The tone curve is the line that says, for each input tone, what it becomes. darktable's manual states it as plainly as anyone: the horizontal axis is the lightness of the input pixels, the vertical axis is the lightness of the output pixels. To read a value, follow it up from the bottom until you meet the curve, then across to the side. The default straight diagonal is the identity: every input maps to the same output, so the picture is unchanged. That diagonal is the reference. Everything you do to an image is a departure from it, above the line to brighten a tone and below it to darken.

The part worth internalizing is what the steepness means. The slope of the curve at a tone is the contrast there. Where the curve rises faster than the diagonal, a small range of input tones is stretched across a wider range of output, so neighboring tones separate and that band gains contrast. Where it rises slower, input tones are squeezed into a narrower output and the band goes flat. Sroyon puts the whole rule in two lines: a steep curve is high contrast, a flat curve is low contrast. This is separation between tones, not the edge-level punch that a clarity or texture slider adds, which comes from looking at a pixel's neighbors rather than from any point on the curve.

## Contrast is a budget, not a gift

Because the two endpoints are usually pinned at the corners, you cannot steepen one part of the curve without flattening another. Cambridge in Colour states it as a law: you can never add contrast in one tonal region without taking it from another, so every picture has a contrast budget you decide how to spend. The classic S-curve spends it the usual way. It pulls the lower midtones down and the upper midtones up, which steepens the middle and adds midtone contrast, and in doing so it flattens the toe and the shoulder, so the deepest shadows and the brightest highlights lose separation. A punchy S looks like more contrast everywhere, but it is really contrast moved into the tones your eye reads first and taken out of the ends.

The Contrast slider is that same S with one handle. RawTherapee describes its contrast control as a curve centered on the average luminance, lifting tones above the midpoint and lowering those below it. That is a rotation of the middle of the curve around a pivot, steeper for more contrast and flatter for less. Knowing that, you can stop treating contrast as a mood knob and start asking the real question the curve forces: which tones do I want to give this separation to, and which ones can afford to lose it.

## Exposure sets the middle, whites and blacks set the ends

Adobe's Basic panel stacks its six tone sliders in a fixed order, Exposure, Contrast, Highlights, Shadows, Whites, Blacks, and the order is a working sequence, not a menu. Exposure comes first because it sets overall brightness, weighted to the midtones while it shifts the whole range, so it places the bulk of the histogram before you shape anything around it. This is worth separating from a real exposure change at capture. Moving the whole curve up in an editor with the endpoints pinned is a protected brightness move, since black stays black and white stays white. Pushing exposure hard enough shifts every level together and can drive tones off either end into clipping. The slider is closer to the first than the second, but the ends are still yours to lose.

Whites and Blacks are the ends. Whites sets the white point, the input level that becomes pure white and above which highlights clip. Blacks sets the black point at the other corner. Highlights and Shadows are their inner cousins: Highlights reshapes the bright tones below the white point without moving it, and Shadows lifts the dark tones above the black point without moving that. This is why the pairs feel similar and are not the same. Lowering Highlights recovers cloud detail while peak white stays put, and setting Whites decides where cloud stops being cloud and becomes paper. The clean way to place the two endpoints is Adobe's own: hold Alt on Windows or Option on a Mac and drag Whites to the right until the first clipped specks appear on the blacked-out preview, then back off a hair. A colored speck means a single channel has clipped, solid white means all three have. Do the mirror move on Blacks against a white preview. On the image itself the J key toggles both clipping overlays at once, blue where shadows have crushed to black and red where highlights have blown to white.

## The two interfaces are one map

The tone curve and the tone sliders are two ways onto the same input-to-output relationship, and it helps to hold them as one. In Lightroom's Tone Curve panel the point curve lets you click control points anywhere, while the parametric curve gives you four region sliders, Highlights, Lights, Darks, and Shadows, over the same line. Drag the parametric curve directly and the matching slider moves with it, which is the clearest proof they act on one mapping. The Basic sliders are looser about it, and Adobe's own descriptions still line up with the shape: Contrast works the midtones like an S around a pivot, Whites and Blacks are the endpoints, and Highlights and Shadows bend the regions just inside them. Treat "the sliders are the curve" as the intuition that makes the panel legible rather than a claim about identical math, and you will reach for whichever handle is faster without losing the plot. ## Recovering the ends is a raw privilege, up to a wall

Highlights that look blown on the back of the camera are often still in the file, because the histogram and the blinkies on the screen are computed from the processed JPEG preview, not from the raw. The raw usually holds something like a stop of headroom above that preview white, sometimes more. Recovery works for a specific physical reason: the three color channels do not saturate at the same instant, so a channel that has clipped can be rebuilt from the ones that still hold data, which is exactly what a raw converter's highlight reconstruction does. That is also why a partly clipped highlight drifts toward magenta if nothing corrects it, since white balance multiplies each channel differently and an overexposed patch with one channel maxed out no longer balances to neutral.

The wall is real and it is hard. When all three channels saturate, the tone is solid white and there is nothing left to reconstruct. Cambridge in Colour says it without hedging: detail can never be recovered once a region becomes so overexposed that it turns pure white. The shadow end has a different limit. You can lift shadows a long way, but the noise floor is the bottom of the range, and the signal-to-noise ratio is worst in the darkest tones, so lift far enough and grain surfaces before detail does. This is the [dynamic range](/iso-sensor-and-dynamic-range) of the file in practice: highlights capped by sensor saturation at the top, shadows floored by read noise at the bottom, and your edit living in between. It is why exposing to the right, pushing the histogram as far right as you can without clipping and pulling it back in the develop, gives the tone edit the most to work with, and why [metering](/metering-and-exposure-decisions) and the develop are the same decision made twice.

The histogram is where you read all of this, with one caution. A pile of pixels against a wall is clipping, tones sent to pure black or pure white with their detail lost. But a wall that is touched is not always a wall that is wrong. Specular highlights, a sun in the frame, the glint on water and metal, are meant to be pure white, and true black shadow is meant to be black. And because the on-screen histogram is display-referred, darktable keeps a separate raw-overexposed warning precisely because a highlight flagged as clipped on screen may still be sitting recoverable in the raw. Trust the raw over the preview, and reach for the ends deliberately.

## Where the edit happens, and what your two cameras bring

A curve does not behave the same in every space. Lightroom stores the raw in a linear working space but shows you the histogram and applies your curve through an sRGB-style tone response, so the values you edit are perceptual rather than linear. That is why the shadows and midtones are spread across the width of the curve grid instead of jammed into the far left, where they would sit if the axis were linear, and it is what makes a gentle curve feel gentle. Capture One draws the distinction in its own tools: an RGB curve changes brightness but also pushes saturation and can shift hue on a steep S, while its Luma curve changes tonal density alone and leaves color where it was. Capture One also splits the ends across its High Dynamic Range tool, with Highlight and Shadow reaching into the midtones and White and Black working only the extremes, and keeps the black and white points in its Levels tool. Hasselblad's Phocus renders the 3FR through the Natural Colour Solution and offers Highlight Recovery and Shadow Fill that reclaim real clipped data conservatively rather than inventing it.

How much room you have at the ends depends on the file. Leica quotes the Q3 at up to 14 stops of dynamic range in a 14-bit DNG at base ISO 100. Hasselblad quotes the X2D II 100C at 15.3 stops in 16-bit at base ISO 50, though Bill Claff's independent photographic dynamic range, measured to a stricter, print-normalized noise floor rather than the engineering floor makers use, puts it closer to 12.5 stops. The maker numbers and the measured numbers disagree because they define the bottom of the range differently, and both are worth knowing. The practical point is modest and real: the medium-format file has about a stop more measured room at the ends and finer bit depth to subdivide it with, so it takes a harder pull, a deeper shadow lift or a more aggressive highlight recovery, before the ends give out. On either camera the wall is in the same place. It is only a little farther away on the Hasselblad.

<Callout>
The tone curve maps input brightness to output brightness, and the diagonal is no change. Its slope is contrast, and contrast is a budget: steepen the middle and the ends flatten, so adding punch always costs the shadows and highlights some separation. Set the ends first, since Blacks and Whites define where clipping starts and an Alt or Option drag places them at the edge of it, then shape the middle with exposure and contrast. Highlights and Shadows recover detail inside those ends, and raw gives you room to recover because the three channels do not clip at once, up to the wall where all three saturate and the tone is gone for good. Read the histogram, but trust the raw over the display-referred warning. A tone against the wall is the one thing no curve can bring back.
</Callout>

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Spend the contrast budget on purpose">
Take one frame and add a strong S-curve, or push the Contrast slider well up. Watch the midtones snap into separation, then look hard at the deepest shadows and the brightest highlights and name what they lost. Now flatten the S and add your contrast only in the tonal band that carries the subject, leaving the ends alone. Same picture, two ways of spending the same budget. Decide which tones earned the contrast.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Set the ends by the clipping preview">
On a frame with a full range, hold Alt or Option and drag Whites right until the first colored specks appear on the black preview, then back off until they just vanish. Do the mirror move on Blacks against the white preview. Let go and look at the histogram: it should now reach both walls without piling against either. You have placed the two endpoints of the curve by eye, at the threshold where detail begins to clip.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Find the highlight wall">
Find a frame whose sky the camera flagged as blown. Pull Highlights down, then Whites, and watch how much detail was actually still in the raw. Keep going until a patch stops responding and stays flat white no matter what you do. That patch is where all three channels saturated. Everything up to it was recoverable, and it was not. Now you know what the wall feels like from the inside.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="04" title="Lift a shadow until it breaks">
Take a dark frame and raise Shadows, then Blacks, opening the darkest corner. Push until grain surfaces and the detail turns to mush, then ease back to the last setting that still looked clean. That point is the bottom of your usable range on this file, and it will sit lower on the base-ISO Hasselblad frame than on a pushed high-ISO one. The noise floor, not the slider, decides how far down you can reach.
</ExerciseCard>
