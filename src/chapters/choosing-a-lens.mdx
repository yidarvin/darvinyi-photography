Ask what lens to use and the honest answer is a question back: what is the picture about, and how much of the world belongs in it. A focal length is not a look you buy. It is a job. It sets how wide a slice of the scene lands on the sensor, and through the distance it pushes you to stand at, it sets what the frame is about. The [last chapter](/lenses-optics-and-perspective) took a lens apart and found only two jobs inside it, the slice it keeps and the sharpness it renders, with perspective belonging to your feet. This chapter puts the lens back together as a working decision. The two cameras in this book answer that decision in opposite ways. The Leica Q3 hands you one focal length, a 28mm, and asks you to walk and to crop. The Hasselblad X2D opens onto a whole ladder of XCD glass and asks you to pick and to carry. Because focal length only chooses a slice, both routes reach the same framings. What differs is what you commit to, and what each one charges.

## A focal length is a job

Group the focal lengths by what they are for, in full-frame-equivalent terms so the two systems speak the same language. A wide lens, roughly 24 to 35mm, keeps you close and keeps the context: it is the reportage band, wide enough to hold a subject and the world around it in one frame. Garry Winogrand worked a 28mm on a Leica M4 for his tilted, crowded New York streets, and Daido Moriyama still shoots a fixed 28mm Ricoh GR from the hip. A step longer, 35mm, lets you stand back a pace and stay engaged, the balanced middle Alex Webb builds his layered color frames on.

A normal lens, near 50mm, renders the honest slice, close to what the eye reads as natural, so the picture succeeds or fails on the moment and the composition rather than on any lens effect. Henri Cartier-Bresson made a 50mm his near-exclusive tool because, in his words, it "corresponds to a certain vision and at the same time has enough depth of focus, a thing you don't have in longer lenses." A short telephoto, 70 to 105mm, narrows the angle so you can keep only what you want, and the working distance it forces flatters a face and lifts a subject off its background. Past 135mm the lens reaches for what you cannot or should not approach, and stacks the planes into compression. Below 24mm the ultrawide puts the viewer inside the scene and asks the most of you, because everything, edge to edge, is now in the frame.

None of these is a look the glass adds. Each is the distance the focal length makes you work at, which is the [previous chapter's](/lenses-optics-and-perspective) point wearing work clothes. Choosing a focal length is choosing where you will stand and how much you will keep. Everything else follows.

## The Q3 answer: one lens and your feet

The Q3 settles the question by not offering it. A fixed Summilux 28mm f/1.7 ASPH is bonded to the body, eleven elements in nine groups with three aspherical surfaces, stabilized, and able to focus down to 17cm in macro. You cannot change it, and that is the design, not a limitation of it. A single focal length, lived with, stops being a constraint and becomes a way of seeing: you learn to frame the shot before the camera reaches your eye, because you already know what 28mm will keep. The one-lens tradition runs straight through the photographers above, each married to a number.

To reach a longer framing on the Q3 you have two moves. You can walk in, which changes the framing and, because it changes your distance, the perspective as well. Or you can crop, and the Q3 builds the crop into the finder as framelines at 35, 50, 75, and 90mm. These are not extra lenses. They are slices of the one 28mm frame, and the resolution falls as you take less of it: 60 megapixels at 28mm, then about 39 at 35, 19 at 50, 8 at 75, and 6 at 90. The full 60-megapixel raw is written whatever the frameline, with the crop stored only as metadata, so in software you can reset a DNG back to the whole sensor. The crop is a preview you commit to only in the JPEG. What the crop cannot do is give you a view wider than 28mm, or move the perspective, because both of those live in your feet. And 90mm is the ceiling.

## The X2D answer: pick from the ladder

The X2D takes the other road: a mount, and a range of XCD lenses to hang on it. Because the sensor is larger than full frame, focal lengths read longer than their number, and the [crop factor](/aperture-and-depth-of-field) is 0.79, so any XCD focal length times 0.79 gives its full-frame framing. The modern spine of the range is the V-series of primes, all f/2.5: the 25V frames like a 20mm wide, the 38V like a 30mm, the 55V like a 43mm, which is the system's normal lens, and the 90V like a 71mm, the portrait length. Each is a piece of glass optimized for one focal length, and each records the full 100 megapixels.

Around that spine sit the choices. The P-series pancakes, the 28P, 45P, and 75P, trade a little speed for a flat, light kit you can carry all day. Two zooms cover the ground without a lens change, the 20-35E at the wide end and the newer 35-100E through the standard range. The specialists are there when the picture needs them: the 80mm f/1.9, the fastest lens Hasselblad has ever made for the system, for the shallowest rendering; the 120mm f/3.5 Macro, framing like a 95mm, for close work; and the 135mm f/2.8 with its 1.7x converter for reach. Every one keeps the full 100 megapixels, and every one costs a lens change and weight in the bag. On the X2D you buy the focal length you intend to live at.

## Two ways along one ladder

Lay the two systems on a single axis of framing and the trade is plain. Both reach from the wide end to the short telephoto, but the Q3 does it from one point by cropping, spending pixels as it reaches, while the X2D does it with separate lenses that each hold the full frame. The Q3 cannot go wider than its 28mm; the X2D's ladder starts wider, at the 25V's 20mm-equivalent, and climbs at full resolution the whole way.

## Prime or zoom

Once you can change lenses, the older question returns: a fixed prime or a zoom. Primes are usually faster, smaller, lighter, and cheaper for the light they gather, and at the edges of the frame they still tend to win. Roger Cicala's testing at LensRentals put it plainly, that a good zoom stopped down is "not as good as the inexpensive prime, at least at the edges, but close enough for most people most of the time." A zoom pays for that with flexibility: it reframes without moving your feet, which is what saves the shot when the moment will not wait, at an event or with a child or on the street.

The deeper split is discipline against convenience. A prime makes you move, and moving is how you learn what 35mm feels like as a position relative to your subject rather than a number on a barrel. The Q3 makes this choice for you and enforces it. The X2D leaves it open, and either answer is defensible. The one trap is copying a master's number instead of their eye. Cartier-Bresson was great because of how he saw, not because of the 50. Pick the focal length that matches how you see, then learn it until you no longer think about it.

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Shoot a day at one framing">
Pick a single focal length and stay there for a day. On the Q3, lock the 28mm and leave the framelines alone, or fix the 50mm crop and never touch it. On the X2D, mount one prime and leave the rest at home. Do not zoom with your feet to fix every frame; instead learn where you have to stand for the picture you want. By the afternoon you will start seeing the frame before you raise the camera, which is the whole point of a fixed field of view.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Map your own frames to a focal length">
Look back through a few hundred of your own pictures and note the focal length of the ones you actually like. On a fixed-lens camera, note which crop frameline they used. A pattern almost always shows up: most photographers keep returning to one or two framings. That cluster is the lens you should learn first, and on the X2D it tells you which prime to buy before any other.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Crop until it hurts, then count the pixels">
Take one 28mm Q3 frame and crop it in software to the 50mm and then the 90mm frameline. Watch the file fall from 60 megapixels toward 6 and decide, for the size you actually print or post, where the crop stops being free. That number is your real reach on the Q3, and it is the honest reason the X2D's dedicated glass exists: the same framing, without spending a single pixel.
</ExerciseCard>
