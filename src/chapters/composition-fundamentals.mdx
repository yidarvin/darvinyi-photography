A photograph is a rectangle you fill on purpose. The scene in front of you does not arrange itself; you decide, in the finder, where each thing sits inside the frame, and that decision is the whole of composition. It is not a layer of polish added after the picture is made. It is the control that sets where a viewer's eye lands first, where it travels next, and where it comes to rest. Everything filed under composition, the thirds and the lines and the frames and the empty space, is one job wearing many names: steering attention. This chapter names the tools and the single principle they all serve.

## The eye is not neutral

Before you can place anything, you have to know what the eye does on its own, because it does a great deal before you get a vote. It is not a scanner that records the frame evenly. Only your central vision is sharp; by twenty degrees off center the eye resolves about a tenth as much detail, and at the edges it catches only broad contrast and almost no color. So the eye builds a picture by jumping, fixing on one region of interest after another, and the detailed scene you feel you saw was, in Cambridge in Colour's phrase, prioritized based on interest. Composition works by deciding what counts as interesting.

What pulls the gaze, before any conscious thought, is a fast and memory-free layer of vision that reacts to whatever stands out from its surroundings. This is salience, and it arises from contrast between a region and its neighborhood. A few things win that competition almost every time. The brightest, highest-contrast patch usually takes the eye first, which is the composition side of everything in [reading light](/reading-light). Faces take it harder still: the brain has a region dedicated to them, and in free viewing people look at faces roughly sixteen times more than matched parts of the same image, and struggle to look away even when told to. A visible eye, and the direction it points, pulls hardest of all. The odd one out pulls too, instantly and regardless of how much surrounds it, so one red berry among green leaves or one lit face in a dark crowd jumps forward on its own; warm, saturated color pulls more than muted color. And a sharp subject against a soft background reads as the subject, which is the attention lever behind [aperture and depth of field](/aperture-and-depth-of-field). Composition is the craft of aiming these pulls where you want them, rather than letting them fight you.

## Off center is where the frame wakes up

The oldest placement tip is the rule of thirds. The English painter John Thomas Smith coined the phrase in 1797, writing that he had found the ratio of about two thirds to one third a more harmonizing proportion than the precise formal half. Picture two evenly spaced lines across the frame and two down it, cutting the picture into nine equal parts. Put your important elements along those lines and your subject where they cross. The four crossings are often called power points, and a subject set on one, a head, a flower, an animal's eye, tends to hold attention better than the same subject dropped in the middle. For a landscape it means the horizon rides the upper or lower third instead of splitting the picture into two dead halves.

The reason it works is that it defeats the reflex to center. Dead center is stable and symmetric, and stability reads as calm, but past a point it also reads as inert, with the eye parked and nowhere to go. Nudge the subject off center and you introduce a small imbalance the eye wants to resolve, which is another way of saying you make the picture move. Treat the rule as a habit, not a law. It was doubted from the start: George Field wrote in 1845 that it does not supply a general law but universalises a particular, and that following it blindly yields a uniform and monotonous practice. Cambridge in Colour puts it plainly, that it is just a rough guideline. A close cousin, the golden-ratio phi grid, places its lines a little nearer the center, but the value of either is the same: it makes you ask where the subject should sit instead of centering it by default.

## Lines are a path you lay for the eye

Lines are the artist's way of directing the eye, and the eye follows a strong one the way a finger follows a wire. It wants to run along the line to see where it ends. A road, a fence, a shoreline, a river, a handrail: place one so it enters near a corner and runs toward your subject, and you have built a path the viewer walks without noticing. Lines carry feeling as well as direction. Horizontals read as calm and spacious, verticals as height and grandeur, and diagonals as movement and energy, with tightly converging lines feeling the most dynamic of all.

Convergence does a second job. As parallel lines run away from you they appear to meet at a vanishing point, and that apparent meeting is how a flat photograph fakes depth, pulling the eye from the foreground into the distance. It is the same perspective effect that reshapes a scene in [lenses, optics, and perspective](/lenses-optics-and-perspective). The catch is that a line is honest to a fault: it delivers the eye wherever it points. Aim it at your subject and it hands the viewer straight to it. Aim it at an empty corner and it walks the viewer out of the frame.

## A frame inside the frame

Look for something in the scene that wraps around your subject: a doorway, a window, an arch, an overhanging branch, a gap in a wall. Shoot through it and you get a frame inside the frame, and it does three things at once. It funnels the eye through the opening to the subject and walls off the surrounding clutter. It adds depth, because the near frame and the far subject sit on obviously different planes. And it lends context or mood, the borrowed meaning of looking through one thing at another. The frame need not be neat or complete. A branch across one corner or an arch cutting the top works as well as a full border, and both man-made openings, windows and doors and mirrors, and natural ones, branches and foliage and cave mouths, serve the same end.

## Empty space is doing work

The room around your subject is not wasted. It is negative space, the counterpart to the positive space of the subject, and the interaction of the two is what makes a composition work rather than either one alone. Give a subject a wide, quiet field and you do two useful things at once. You isolate it, so nothing competes for the eye, and you let it breathe. The clearest instruction that falls out of this is lead room, also called nose room or looking room: leave space in the direction the subject faces or moves. When the eye first scans a photograph it expects to see a little in front of the subject. A car with room ahead of it is going somewhere; a car pinned against the front edge has been stopped. A person looking left belongs on the right of the frame, gazing across the open space, not jammed into the left edge staring out of it. Deny that room and the frame turns uneasy. The cinematographer Neil Oseman compares short-siding a subject to a person who walks into a room and stands facing the wall, close enough to touch it: you feel something is wrong before you can say what.

## Balance is the sum of all of it

Under every one of these devices sits a single principle. Every element in a frame carries a visual weight, a measure of how strongly it attracts the eye, and that weight comes from the same things that drew the eye in the first place: size, tonal and color contrast, saturation, sharpness, isolation, and above all faces and eyes. A large dim shape can weigh the same as a small brilliant one. A frame feels resolved when those weights balance around its center, and the balance behaves like a see-saw. An element's pull is its weight times its distance from the center, so a large, quiet mass near the middle is balanced by a small, loud accent placed far out toward the edge.

That one equation explains the rest. It is why a subject on a third does not tip the picture over: the open space on the long side has weight of its own and sits far from the center, balancing the subject on the short side. Symmetry, the same weight mirrored on both halves, is the easy kind of balance, formal and stable and, past a point, static. Asymmetry, a small strong thing set against a large soft thing, is harder to see and more alive, and it is the balance most photographs are reaching for.

<Callout>
The named devices are one idea in different clothes. The eye goes first to contrast, to faces, and to whatever differs from its surroundings, and composition is arranging those pulls on purpose. Put the subject off the dead center. Lay a line toward it. Frame it if the scene offers a frame. Leave it room to look into. Then check that the weights balance around the center, formally when you want calm and asymmetrically when you want life. Do that and you are deciding where the eye lands, instead of hoping it lands well.
</Callout>

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Catch the first fixation">
Take ten photographs you admire, your own or others', and for each note the very first place your eye landed before you studied anything. It will almost always be the brightest patch, a face, or the one thing unlike its surroundings. Then ask whether that is where the photographer wanted you to look. You are learning to see salience, the raw material every other move in this chapter steers.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Center it, then move it to a third">
Pick one subject and make two frames without changing anything else: one with the subject dead center, one with it on a thirds intersection and open space on the far side. Put them side by side. The centered frame will look like a document and the off-center one will look composed. Repeat it until off-center is your reflex and centering has become a deliberate choice.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Aim a line at your subject">
Find a strong line in the world, a road, a rail, a fence, a long shadow, and stand so it enters a bottom corner and runs to your subject. Take the shot. Then take one where the same line runs to an empty corner instead. The first hands the eye to the subject; the second loses it in the edge. The line never changed, only where you pointed it.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="04" title="Give it room to look">
Photograph a person or animal in profile twice: once with generous space in the direction they face, once crammed against the edge they face, nothing else different. The first breathes and the second feels trapped. Lead room is the cheapest way there is to make a portrait feel intentional rather than accidental.
</ExerciseCard>
