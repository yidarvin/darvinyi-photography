A flash needs one thing from the shutter: a single instant when the whole frame is open at once. On most cameras that instant exists only up to a certain speed, the flash sync speed, somewhere around 1/200 or 1/250 of a second. Fire a flash any faster and part of the frame comes back black. Both cameras in this book are built so that instant never goes away. The Leica Q3 puts its shutter inside the lens, and every Hasselblad XCD lens carries its own, so both open the entire frame together at every speed and sync a flash at all of them. [How a shutter uncovers the frame](/how-a-shutter-uncovers-the-frame) sorted the four shutter types by exactly this question. This chapter is about what the answer buys you, and it comes down to one move a leaf shutter can make that an ordinary camera cannot: drop a flash into bright sun, at a wide aperture, at full power, and hold the sky down behind your subject.

## The sync wall

Most cameras with a lens mount put a focal-plane shutter right in front of the sensor: two curtains that cross the frame one after the other. The first slides open to start the exposure, the second follows to close it. Up to a certain speed the first curtain fully clears the frame before the second starts to move, so for a moment the whole sensor sits open. That moment is what a flash needs. The fastest speed at which it still happens is the sync speed, and it is nothing more than the time the curtain takes to cross the frame, around 1/200 to 1/250 on a full-frame body, 1/320 on a few.

Past that speed the moment is gone. To expose faster the second curtain has to launch before the first has finished, so the two run together with a gap between them, and only that moving slit is ever open. A flash burst is far too short to bridge it. The burst lasts on the order of a thousandth of a second, and less at low power, as short as a thirty-thousandth, while the curtains need several milliseconds to cross. So the flash fires while the slit is partway down the frame and lights only the strip it happens to be crossing. The rest of the frame, still behind a curtain, records nothing. You get a hard dark band across the top or the bottom, the unmistakable sign of a flash fired past its sync speed.

An electronic shutter fails the same way and for the same reason. It uncovers the sensor row by row over the readout time, so there is no instant when every row is collecting light, and a single burst stripes the frame exactly as the slit does. That is why [rolling shutter and readout](/rolling-shutter-and-readout) keeps both cameras' electronic shutters away from flash entirely. The one electronic shutter that escapes this is the global shutter, which starts and stops every pixel together. The Sony a9 III, the first full-frame camera to carry one, syncs a flash all the way to 1/80000. The rule is that clean: open the whole frame at once and the flash works, uncover it progressively and it does not.

## The leaf shutter has no wall

A leaf shutter has no curtains and no slit. It is a ring of thin blades sitting at the aperture, inside the lens, and to make an exposure they spring open from the center outward, uncover the whole opening at once, hold, then close back the same way. There is never a moment when part of the frame is covered and part is not. Light reaches every corner of the sensor over the same interval at every speed, so a single full-power flash burst lights the entire frame whether the shutter is set to 1/15 or 1/2000. That is the whole of the leaf advantage, and it is why neither camera in this book has a sync speed to worry about.

Both cameras are built around it. The Q3's shutter lives in its fixed 28mm lens and syncs a flash at every mechanical speed up to its top of 1/2000. The X2D II has no mechanical shutter in its body at all; the blades are in the lens, one leaf shutter per XCD, and they sync flash at every speed too. Most XCD lenses reach 1/2000, and four of the newest, the 25V, the 90V, the 28P, and the 35-100mm zoom, reach 1/4000. This is not a quirk of these two cameras. Leaf-shutter medium-format bodies have always synced fast, Phase One backs to somewhere between 1/1600 and 1/2500, and it is the reason studio and location photographers have reached for them for decades.

## Two exposures in one frame

Here is the idea that makes fast flash sync worth having. Every photograph made with flash is two exposures stacked on top of each other: one of the ambient light already in the scene, and one of the light the flash adds. They share the frame but they answer to different controls. The aperture sets both, because it is the same opening for both kinds of light. The shutter sets only the ambient. A flash fires and is over in well under a millisecond, so once the frame has been open long enough to catch the whole burst, holding it open longer does nothing to the flash and everything to the ambient. Change the [shutter speed](/shutter-speed-and-motion) and you brighten or darken the light already in the scene while the flash-lit subject does not move.

That one fact is the lever. Set the aperture and the flash power to light your subject, then use the shutter to place the background wherever you want it, bright, neutral, or dark. Bright sun gives you the most room to work. Sunny-16 says a sunlit scene at ISO 100 wants about f/16 at 1/125, the same exposure as f/11 at 1/250, f/8 at 1/500, f/5.6 at 1/1000, f/4 at 1/2000. Pin the aperture at f/8, where the sky is correct at 1/500, and from there every faster stop of shutter pulls the sky down one stop, to 1/1000, then 1/2000, without touching the flash-lit subject. The figure holds one aperture and one flash and walks the shutter across that ladder: the subject stays lit while the sky slides from blown white down toward dark, and the only question left is how fast a shutter your flash will sync at.

## Overpowering the sun

Put the lever and the wall together and the payoff is plain. Say you want a portrait in full sun at f/8 with a deep, saturated sky rather than a pale washed-out one. To hold that sky two stops down you need 1/2000, and you need the flash to light the face at that speed. On the Q3 or an XCD lens you set 1/2000 and fire; the leaf shutter syncs and the flash lands at full power. On a focal-plane camera the flash quits at 1/250, where that same f/8 leaves the sky a stop brighter than neutral, three stops brighter than the deep sky you asked for, and the shutter cannot go faster without the black band. Three stops of sky, the whole run from 1/250 to 1/2000, belong to the leaf shutter alone. A focal-plane camera's only ways around the wall are to stop the lens down and give up the [wide aperture](/aperture-and-depth-of-field), to screw a neutral-density filter over the front and dim everything at once, or to switch to high-speed sync and pay for it in flash power.

## High-speed sync, and its price

High-speed sync is the focal-plane camera's answer to the wall, and it is a clever one. Above the sync speed, when only a slit is ever open, the flash stops firing a single burst and instead pulses tens of thousands of times a second for the whole time the slit takes to cross the frame. Every strip of the sensor is lit as the slit passes over it, and the rapid pulses blur together into what looks like one even flash. Nikon calls it Auto FP, a name inherited from the FP flashbulbs of the film era that burned slowly enough to do the same job. It genuinely works, and it lets an ordinary camera shoot flash at 1/8000.

It just costs a great deal of light. The flash is lit for the whole crossing, but any given strip of sensor only ever sees it through the narrow moving slit, so most of the output is spent lighting parts of the frame that are covered at that instant. The penalty runs around two to three stops, and it grows as the shutter gets faster, roughly a stop of flash power for every stop of shutter speed, because a faster shutter means a narrower slit and more pulses spread thinner. A flash that could reach across a room with an ordinary burst reaches only a few feet in high-speed sync: Nikon's own instructors note that a powerful SB-900 at 1/8000 lights a subject to about eight feet and no farther. The leaf shutter gets you to the same fast shutter with a single full-power burst and none of that tax. The Q3 can still fall back on high-speed, or linear, flash with compatible Leica units for a speed past its 1/2000 ceiling, but with a leaf shutter you rarely need to.

## What else the burst gives you

Two smaller gifts come with the flash. The first is a choice of when in the exposure it fires. Trigger it as the shutter opens, called first-curtain or front-curtain sync, and a moving subject's blur trails out ahead of it, which looks wrong. Trigger it just before the shutter closes, rear-curtain sync, and the blur trails behind the subject where motion belongs, so a passing cyclist leaves a streak that reads as speed rather than a mistake. The setting lives in the flash menu on both cameras.

The second is a kind of speed you cannot get any other way. A flash at low power is over in as little as a thirty-thousandth of a second, far shorter than any mechanical shutter can manage. In a dim room, where the ambient records almost nothing during the exposure, the flash becomes the real shutter: it, not the blades, decides how long the subject is lit, and it freezes a splash or a wingbeat sharper than 1/2000 ever could. That is why a water-droplet photograph is made with a weak flash in a dark room, not a fast shutter in a bright one.

## Feel it

The widget puts you behind a camera in bright sun at f/8, with the flash already set to light the subject. Your one move is the shutter, and your one choice is the kind of shutter. Start on the leaf shutter and raise the speed: the sky darkens a stop at a time while the subject holds, all the way to a deep sky at 1/2000. Now switch to the focal-plane shutter at the same speed and watch the frame break, the flash collapsing to a bright band with the rest gone dark, because past 1/250 there is no instant when the whole frame is open. Slow the focal-plane shutter back to its sync speed and the sky is stuck a stop too bright, with nowhere to go. The gap between where the two shutters give out is the whole chapter.

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Find your own black band">
On any focal-plane camera, a borrowed DSLR or mirrorless body will do, put a flash in the hot shoe and shoot at 1/1000. The frame comes back with a dark band across one edge where the shutter had not finished opening. Slow down until the band disappears; the speed where it vanishes is that camera's sync speed. Then pick up the Q3 or the X2D, do the same at 1/2000, and there is no band at any speed. You have measured the wall, then watched a leaf shutter not have one.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Move the sky with the shutter">
Set up a portrait in open shade or sun with a flash lighting the face. Fix the aperture and the flash power so the face is right, then shoot the same frame at 1/125, 1/500, and 1/2000 without changing anything else. The face stays put across all three while the background walks from bright to dark. That is the shutter controlling the ambient and nothing else, the single fact the whole technique rests on.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Overpower the sun">
In bright sun, meter the sky, then set your aperture for the depth of field you want and raise the shutter one or two stops past the metered ambient so the sky goes deliberately dark. Bring the flash up to relight the subject at that aperture. On a leaf shutter you can push the shutter well past 1/250 to bury the sky; on a focal-plane camera you hit the sync speed first and have to choose between a bright sky, a smaller aperture, and high-speed sync. Shoot it and see which wall you reach.
</ExerciseCard>
