A raw develop is a short list of moves. Correct the frame, set the white balance, place the exposure, shape the contrast, work the color, then dodge and burn and sharpen. The moves look independent, a rack of sliders you could pull in any order. They are not. Each one assumes the moves before it are already settled, so the list has a natural order, and that order is a chain of dependencies rather than a matter of taste. Work down the chain and every decision gets made once. Jump around it and you keep re-deciding the same things, because a step you set early quietly changes under a step you set late. [Ingest and catalog](/ingest-and-catalog) got the negative onto disk and into a library, and [the digital negative](/the-digital-negative) is the file you are now going to develop. This chapter is the map for the rest of Part III: the order the develop wants to happen in, and why fighting that order is the most common way an edit turns into a mess.

## A stack, not a rack of sliders

The panels in a raw editor sit side by side and look like peers, any one as reachable as the next. The logic underneath is a stack. White balance decides what counts as neutral, so every color decision rests on it. Exposure sets how bright the whole frame sits, so the contrast curve is drawn around a midpoint that exposure already put in place. A local mask darkens one corner of a picture whose global look you have already chosen. Pull a lower slider after you have set the ones above it and the upper decisions no longer fit the thing they were deciding about, so you make them again.

Two rules fall out of that structure, and between them they cover most of the order. Work from the whole frame inward, settling the global look before you touch anything local. And work from the frame outward first, fixing the shape of the picture, its geometry and its crop, before you judge tone or place a mask on it. Photographers call the combination working outside in, or global before local, and every published develop order is a version of it.

## The engine reorders the math for you

None of this feels dangerous in practice, and it is worth saying why so the rest of the chapter is about judgment and not fear. A modern raw editor is parametric. It never rewrites the negative. It keeps your edits as a list of settings and recomputes the picture from the raw every time, applying them in its own fixed internal order no matter what order you touched the controls. darktable states this outright in its manual: the module order is predefined, and the order in which you switch modules on has no impact on the outcome, a sequence chosen to respect real technical dependencies rather than to match the panel layout. Adobe is quieter about its exact internal sequence, which it has never fully published, but its own guidance tells you that you can make Develop adjustments in any order and only recommends starting at the top of the Basic panel and working down. Move exposure, then white balance, then exposure again, and you land in the same place as someone who worked in textbook order. Your Leica's DNG and your Hasselblad's 3FR go through the same parametric treatment, whether in Lightroom, in Capture One, or in Hasselblad's Phocus before you round-trip a sixteen-bit TIFF onward.

So the order is not there to protect the file. You cannot break a raw by pulling sliders out of sequence. It is there to protect your time and your judgment. Each step is easier to judge against a settled base, and setting the base last means judging everything above it twice. That is the entire payoff, and it is why order of operations here is a working discipline rather than a rule the software enforces. The convention is not even universal. Capture One stacks its Color tools above Exposure, the reverse of Lightroom's tone-first emphasis, and both are fine, because in both the engine has the last word on the math.

## Set the base before you build on it

Start with the frame itself. Lens corrections and geometry come first because they physically move the picture. Turning on a lens profile, removing chromatic aberration, then straightening verticals with a perspective or Upright correction warps and rescales the frame, and Adobe's own Julieanne Kost notes that choosing an Upright mode resets any crop and manual perspective you had already dialed in. Correct the frame, commit the crop, and only then do you have a stable rectangle to compose within. Crop first and the geometry that follows can pull in content you cut or push out content you meant to keep. This is also why the lens matters upstream of the edit, and why [lens corrections](/lenses-optics-and-perspective) belong to the file long before you reach for a mask.

With the frame fixed, set the color foundation. [White balance](/color-science-for-photographers) comes early for a plain reason: it defines neutral, and getting it right, as Cambridge in Colour puts it, often makes the single biggest improvement to a photo's color. Every saturation and grading choice after it is judged against that neutral, so a white balance set late means the color work you already did was compensating for a cast instead of refining the picture. Exposure comes next, before contrast, because exposure places the overall tonal scale and its endpoints, while a contrast curve is an S drawn around a midpoint. Fix the endpoints first or you are drawing [the curve](/tone-and-the-curve) against a baseline that keeps sliding.

Then, and only then, go local. A mask refines the global result and is tuned against it, so it assumes the global look is done. Set your global exposure after you have masked and every mask is now aimed at the wrong brightness and has to be retuned. Reach for [local edits](/local-adjustments-and-masking) to fix a global problem and, as one Lightroom teacher puts it, you end up patching global problems with local edits and building complexity that is hard to repeat and harder to troubleshoot. Global before local pays off in retuning you never have to do: settle the whole frame once, and each mask gets tuned a single time.

## Detail is the last thing, and it comes in order too

The finish, noise reduction and sharpening, sits at the bottom of the stack for a reason close to physics. The two are near opposites. Sharpening raises local contrast to make edges crisper, which also makes noise louder. Noise reduction smooths noise, which also softens real detail. Run them the wrong way round and they sabotage each other. Sharpen a [noisy file](/iso-sensor-and-dynamic-range) first and you have amplified the grain into something that reads like detail, so the noise reduction that follows smears the real detail trying to remove it. Denoise first, then sharpen. And because contrast and tonal pushes make noise worse as well, the whole detail pass belongs after tone and color are set, working on data that already reflects the final look. Where exactly noise reduction sits within that finish is debated, some editors denoise a little earlier and some later, but the two invariants hold everywhere: denoise before you sharpen, and leave detail until after tone and color.

Sharpening in particular is not one step but three, in the model Bruce Fraser laid out and most editors still follow. A light capture pass undoes the softening every sensor and lens introduce. An optional creative pass adds bite to chosen areas. An output pass is tuned to the final file. Output sharpening comes dead last because it depends on things that are not settled until the end, the exact pixel dimensions and the medium. As Cambridge in Colour notes, sharpening is size, resolution, and medium dependent, and resampling a picture down to under about half its size strips the sharpening halos back out, so any sharpening you did before the final resize is thrown away. Crop and resize to [the output](/output-and-print), sharpen for that output, then export. Sharpen earlier and you are sharpening a picture that will not exist at that size.

## When the order is baked in

Everything above assumes a parametric editor that recomputes from the raw. Step outside it, into pixel editing in Photoshop, and the forgiveness ends. There the operations write to the pixels in the literal order you apply them, and some cannot be undone once the file is flattened or resized. The discipline becomes stricter: keep as much reversible for as long as you can, working on Smart Objects and adjustment layers so transforms and filters stay editable, and save the irreversible moves, flatten, resize, and output sharpen, for the very end. It is the same principle with harder enforcement. The parametric order is a strong suggestion. The destructive one is a door that only opens one way.

<Callout>
A raw develop is a stack, and each step assumes the ones before it are settled. That gives a natural order: correct the geometry and crop, set white balance, place exposure, shape contrast, work color, go local, then finish with noise reduction and sharpening sized to the output. A parametric editor applies its own fixed pipeline no matter what order you touch the sliders, so you cannot break the file, but you can waste your afternoon. Set the base last and you judge everything above it twice. Global before local, outside in, detail last, output sharpening after the final resize. Work down the stack and each decision is made once.
</Callout>

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Break global-before-local on purpose">
Develop a frame in order to a look you like, with a couple of local masks doing real work. Now go back to the top and shove the global exposure a full stop either way. Watch the contrast curve and every mask fall out of place at once, each one now aimed at a brightness that no longer exists. Undo. You have just felt, in one move, why the base is set before anything is built on it.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Time two develops of the same file">
Take one frame and develop it strictly top down, geometry to detail, and time yourself. Develop a second frame by jumping around, mask first, white balance last, sharpening somewhere in the middle, and time that. The parametric engine gets you to the same final look either way, so the pictures can match. Notice how much longer the scattered pass took and how many settings you touched twice to get there.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Denoise before you sharpen">
Find a high-ISO frame with visible grain. Sharpen it hard first, then add strong noise reduction, and look closely at the mush where fine detail used to be. Reset. Now denoise first, then sharpen. Compare the two at one hundred percent. Nothing changed but the order, and the order was the whole difference.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="04" title="Sharpen for the size you export">
Export a full-size frame with your normal sharpening. Then take a copy, resize it small for the web, and export it without touching sharpening. Then take a third, resize it the same way, and add output sharpening tuned for that small size. Compare all three at their real display size. The last one is the reason output sharpening waits until after the resize.
</ExerciseCard>
