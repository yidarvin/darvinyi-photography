[Where the eye lands](/composition-fundamentals) was about a single decision: where the subject sits, and where the eye goes first. This chapter is about everything else in the rectangle. Once the subject is placed, the rest of the frame is not background to be tolerated. It is material to be organized, and the eye reads all of it. The advanced work of composition is arranging the whole frame so the eye moves through it on a path you chose, feels the picture as one deep and unified thing rather than a pile of parts, and stays inside the border instead of wandering out. Under the named techniques, tension and geometry and gestalt and depth, sits one fact about the viewer: the mind cannot look at a frame without organizing it. Your job is to steer that organizing rather than fight it.

## The mind insists on order

Gestalt psychology began with a train ride. In 1912 the German psychologist Max Wertheimer published a study of apparent motion, the illusion that two lights flashed in quick succession look like one light moving, and the school that grew around him with Wolfgang Köhler and Kurt Koffka set out to explain how the mind builds wholes out of parts. Their central finding, in Koffka's careful phrasing, is that the whole is other than the sum of its parts. Not greater, other. A face is a different kind of thing from a list of features, and you cannot predict it by adding up two eyes, a nose, and a mouth. The rule that governs the building is Prägnanz, the pull of perception toward the simplest, most regular, most stable reading a scene will allow. Show the eye a scatter of marks and it will not hold them as a scatter. It goes looking for the order.

The mind groups by a small set of cues, and every one of them is a composition tool. Things close together read as a unit, which is proximity. Things that match in color or shape or size read together even when they sit far apart, which is similarity. Elements moving the same way read as one body, which is common fate. The eye follows a smooth continuous line and reads whatever lies along it as connected, which is good continuation, the perception behind every leading line. Given a broken outline the eye supplies the missing edge and sees a whole shape, which is closure. A shared boundary binds whatever falls inside it, a pool of light or a frame within the frame, which is common region. And before any of this the field splits into a figure that stands forward and a ground that falls back, the separation you make with focus and contrast and the negative space from [the last chapter](/composition-fundamentals). Edgar Rubin's reversible vase, the pair of profiles that flips to a goblet, is that last piece of machinery caught in the act of switching.

None of these groupings live in the marks. They live in the viewer, triggered by the cue you supply, and a stronger cue overrides a weaker one. Repetition is the plainest case. One carved pillar is a shape, but a row of identical pillars is a rhythm, and the eye binds them into a single colonnade long before it thinks to count them, grouping by similarity because they match and by good continuation because they line up.

## A frame is a path, not a point

Good continuation is worth staying with, because it turns composition from placing a point into drawing a path. In [the fundamentals](/composition-fundamentals) a single leading line handed the eye to the subject and stopped there. Working the whole frame means giving the eye a route through several elements, and where you can manage it, a route that closes so the eye circulates instead of leaking out an edge. Geometry is how you build the route.

The most useful shape is the triangle. Three subjects arranged as a triangle keep the eye traveling between the vertices instead of settling on one, which is the real reason the rule of odds works: an odd group, usually three, resists the static pairing-off of an even one. A triangle sitting on its base reads as stable and grounded, the way a mountain does. Stand it on a point or tilt it off true and it reads as unstable, which is tension you can spend on purpose. Line direction carries reliable feeling too, catalogued by teachers from the Getty on down. Horizontals rest, verticals stand up and lend strength, and diagonals move, because a thing set on a diagonal looks like it is falling or already going. A diagonal is the most energetic straight line you can lay across a frame.

The curve is the diagonal's supple cousin. William Hogarth built a whole aesthetics around it in 1753, naming the S-shaped serpentine the line of beauty and praising the way it leads the eye a pleasing chase along what he called the continuity of variety. The same winding form is the figura serpentinata that Mannerist painters took from Michelangelo. An S-curve, a river or a road or a staircase, carries the eye deep into a frame while keeping it moving the whole way in.

You will be told there is a deeper geometry under all of this, the golden ratio, and that the Parthenon and the nautilus and the human body are secretly built on it. Be skeptical. The mathematician George Markowsky in 1992, and Stanford's Keith Devlin, who calls it design's biggest myth, showed that most of these sightings are read back into the work after the fact, and that the aesthetic story traces not to antiquity but to Adolf Zeising in the 1850s. A nautilus is a logarithmic spiral, but its growth rate is not the golden ratio. Some measurements do land near it, which is precisely the trap, because near a number that turns up so easily, a willing eye rounds toward it. A few artists, Le Corbusier and Dalí among them, used it deliberately. Most golden spirals laid over a photograph are decoration added later. Compose with triangles and diagonals and curves, which are real and do the work, and leave the magic number alone.

## Tension is what keeps it awake

A frame where everything balances perfectly can die of its own calm. The reason is that balance is not stillness but a standoff of forces, and the eye can feel the forces. Rudolf Arnheim, in his 1954 book Art and Visual Perception, argued that we do not see mere shapes and positions but directed tensions, pulls that have both size and direction, organized around a hidden skeleton of the frame: its center, its vertical and horizontal axes, its diagonals. An element at the dead center sits where the pulls cancel, so it comes to rest. Slide it off center and it turns restless, straining back toward a balance it never reaches, and that unresolved straining is tension. Arnheim pushed the idea of literal physical forces further than the evidence supports, so take it as a way of seeing rather than a law of perception, but as a way of seeing it is hard to beat.

This is why a dead-symmetrical frame can feel inert while a lopsided one feels alive: the asymmetry hands the eye something to resolve, and the work of resolving is the sense of life. Tension comes in several coins. A subject shouldered hard against the edge, or sliced by it, refuses the comfortable contained reading and stays charged. Two diagonals set against each other pull the eye into a fight it cannot settle. A figure that will not separate cleanly from its ground keeps the eye trying and failing to lock it down. And the purest form is a gap that almost closes. Michelangelo loaded the whole of the Creation of Adam into the few centimeters that God's outstretched fingertip never crosses to reach Adam's, and the spark of life lives in that space, not in a touch.

In a photograph the gap is often in time rather than space. Henri Cartier-Bresson's decisive moment is tension held for a five-hundredth of a second, the single instant when things in motion lock into a precarious equilibrium that a hair earlier or later would be formless and slack. You are not freezing a calm arrangement. You are catching a taut one at the one moment it holds.

## Depth on a flat sheet

A photograph is flat. Depth is an illusion the camera does not give you, assembled from cues the brain already trusts. The workhorse move is to build the scene in layers, a clear foreground, a middle distance, and a background, so the eye reads front to back instead of skating across one plane. The strongest single cue is overlap: when a near thing covers part of a far thing, the mind fixes which is in front and cannot be talked out of it. Overlap only ranks the planes, though, it does not measure the distance, so it wants help. Familiar things shrinking give the distance, because the eye knows roughly how large a person or a tree should be and reads the small one as far. And the air itself is a cue. Leonardo da Vinci named it around 1500: distant things go paler, lower in contrast, and bluer, because the atmosphere scatters blue light into the space between you and them until a far ridge takes on the color of the sky. He called it the perspective of disappearance, and noticed that objects lose their finest detail first as they recede.

The old landscape painters had a device that switches several of these cues on at once. Put a dark object in the near foreground, usually along an edge, a tree or a rock or a doorway, and it pushes the rest of the scene back behind it and frames the way in. The French called it repoussoir, roughly to push back, and Claude Lorrain and the Dutch masters built painting after painting on a dark foreground opening onto bands of light that fade to a blue horizon. The psychologist James Gibson added the ground beneath all of it: a textured surface whose grain packs finer as it runs away from you reads as receding by that gradient alone. Stack a strong near anchor against a far background and every cue at once, overlap and size and texture and haze, varies across the frame, which is why that pairing makes the deepest pictures.

<Callout>
Four names, one job. The mind is compelled to organize a frame, and you are choosing what it has to work with. Group the parts so they read as a whole, with proximity and similarity and a shared boundary. Route the eye with geometry and an unbroken line, and close the route so the eye stays in. Charge the frame with tension, an off-center weight or an unclosed gap or a pair of opposed diagonals, so it never goes slack. And layer it front to back so the flat sheet reads as deep. [Where the eye lands](/composition-fundamentals) decided the first fixation. This decides everything the eye does after it.
</Callout>

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Trace the skeleton">
Take five photographs you admire and, for each, trace the main elements with a finger. You are looking for the hidden shape: a triangle, a strong diagonal, an S-curve, a path that loops back on itself. Then go make one frame where three subjects sit as a deliberate triangle. Naming the geometry in pictures that already work is how you learn to build it on purpose.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Flat, then deep">
Photograph one scene twice. First with nothing close, everything at a middle distance, the way a snapshot flattens a place. Then move so a near object, a branch, a railing, a rock, overlaps a middle plane and a far one behind it. Put the two frames side by side. The second has depth the first cannot fake, and it cost you only a few steps and a foreground.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Load a gap">
Find two things that nearly meet: two hands, two branches, a bird and the edge it is about to leave. Make one frame with the gap open and one with the gap closed by touch or overlap. The open gap holds tension and the closed one lets it out. You will feel the charge drain the instant the two elements connect.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="04" title="Break your own symmetry">
Set up a dead-centered, symmetric frame, the kind that photographs like a document. Take it. Then nudge one element off the axis, or push the subject hard toward an edge, and take that. The centered version is stable and a little lifeless; the disturbed one wakes up. Learning when you want each is the whole use of tension.
</ExerciseCard>
