Every edit so far has moved the whole frame at once. Exposure, the [curve](/tone-and-the-curve), white balance, the [grade](/color-grading): each one takes hold of every pixel together. But a photograph is a hierarchy of attention, and the eye is not democratic about it. It goes first to the brightest, highest-contrast part of the frame and works down from there. Local adjustments are how you edit that hierarchy directly, and they all reduce to one idea. A local edit is two separate choices multiplied together: a mask, which is the *where*, and an adjustment, which is the *what*. The mask decides which pixels the sliders are allowed to touch. The sliders decide what happens inside it. Brighten the subject, darken everything around it, and the eye follows to the subject. That move is dodging and burning, a darkroom craft about a century old, and the only thing that has changed is that the computer now does the tracing, so your attention goes to the decision instead of the cutting.

## A local edit is a mask times an adjustment

Start with the mask, because it is the part people find slippery. A mask is a grayscale selection laid over the image. Where the mask is white the adjustment applies at full strength, where it is black the adjustment does nothing, and the greys in between give a proportional, partial effect. This is the old printer's rule, white reveals and black conceals, and it is worth saying plainly because it explains feathering, gradients, and range masks all at once: a soft edge is just the mask fading from white to black across a few pixels, so the effect fades with it. The mask carries no look of its own. It is pure geography.

The adjustment is the other half, and it holds no surprises. It is the same develop panel you already know, exposure, contrast, highlights, clarity, even a [curve](/tone-and-the-curve) or a shift in [color](/color-grading), except that it now reaches only the white parts of the mask. That independence is the whole trick. Any mask can carry any adjustment, so the two questions never have to be answered at the same time. First decide where, then decide what, and you can change your mind about either one without redoing the other. On a raw file the pairing costs nothing to keep open, because the mask and its slider values are stored as instructions and applied at conversion, not painted into the pixels. This is the same non-destructive promise the [raw file](/the-digital-negative) makes everywhere else, and it is why local work belongs late in the [order of operations](/order-of-operations): the global moves settle the frame, and the masks correct and shape what is left.

## The where: four ways to draw the same selection

Because the mask is just a grayscale shape, the only real question is how to draw it, and raw editors give you four kinds of tool. They produce the same kind of selection by different means, so the skill is picking the one that gets a clean edge for the least effort.

The first is the **brush**, a freehand selection you paint by hand. In Lightroom it is governed by four controls: Size sets the tip diameter, Feather sets how soft the edge is, Flow sets how fast paint builds with repeated strokes, and Density caps the maximum strength. An Auto Mask option snaps the brush to edges it detects, so a stroke near a shoulder tends to stop at the shoulder. The second is the **gradient**, in two shapes. A linear gradient fades the mask smoothly across the frame, white at one end to black at the other, which is the classic way to hold down a bright sky. A radial gradient does the same in a circle or oval, bright in the middle or bright at the edges, which is how you build a soft spotlight or an off-center vignette. Both are the old graduated and radial filters, now living as masks.

The third kind selects by content rather than by place. A **range mask** builds its selection from the pixels themselves: a Luminance Range grabs everything within a band of brightness, a Color Range grabs everything of a chosen hue, and a Depth Range grabs everything within a band of distance, on files that carry depth data. Point a Luminance Range at a bright sky and it selects the sky and skips the dark ridgeline poking into it, an edge no gradient could follow. The fourth kind is the newest. **AI selections** read the scene and cut the subject for you. Adobe's October 2021 masking overhaul, which arrived in Lightroom Classic 11 and Camera Raw 14, folded the old brush and filters into a single Masking panel and added two one-click selections, Select Subject and Select Sky, both driven by Adobe's Sensei machine learning. Adobe MAX in October 2022 added Select People, Select Objects, and Select Background, and the People mask goes further still, breaking a person into separately selectable parts: facial skin, body skin, eyebrows, the whites of the eyes, the iris and pupil, lips, teeth, and hair, with clothing and facial hair added in 2023.

However you draw them, masks combine with three set operations. **Add** unions two selections, **Subtract** removes one from another, and **Intersect** keeps only their overlap. A single mask can hold several components at once, so a real selection is often a small sentence: select the subject, subtract a brush over his shirt, and you have a mask of just his skin and face; select the sky, intersect it with a luminance range, and you have only the bright part of the sky. The AI did not change what a mask is. It changed how long it takes to make a hard one, which frees you to think about the edit instead of the outline.

## The what, and why brightening aims the eye

The adjustment inside a mask is nothing new, so the interesting question is why so small a move, a little more exposure here and a little less there, changes a picture so much. The answer is in how looking works. Human attention has a fast, involuntary layer that vision scientists call bottom-up or stimulus-driven, and it is pulled toward whatever differs from its surroundings in simple features: brightness, contrast, color, orientation. Christof Koch and Shimon Ullman proposed in 1985 that the brain combines these feature differences into a single map of conspicuousness, a saliency map, and then sends attention to its current peak in a winner-take-all fashion. Laurent Itti, Koch, and Ernst Niebur turned that idea into a working computer model in 1998, building center-surround contrast maps for intensity, color, and orientation and summing them into one saliency map, purely from the image, with no knowledge of what the scene contains. The eye, in other words, has a bias you can predict.

For a photographer this collapses into a rule of thumb worth trusting: the eye goes to the brightest, highest-contrast, most saturated region of the frame. That is the physics behind [where the eye lands](/composition-fundamentals), and it is exactly the lever a local adjustment pulls. Dodge the subject a third of a stop and its luminance and its contrast against the background both rise, so its place on the saliency map rises with them. Burn the surround and every competing region sinks. You are not decorating the photograph. You are editing the map of conspicuousness so its peak sits where you want the viewer to look. The widget makes the connection literal. Push local tone around a small scene and watch the reticle, which marks the eye's likely landing point, move to whatever region you have made bright and distinct.

The lit face in the next frame is this rule with no software at all. A single source rakes across the man's forehead and cheek and falls off fast, so his face is the one bright, high-contrast region in an otherwise black frame. Your eye had no choice. When you dodge a subject and burn a background in the edit, you are reaching for this same tonal separation after the fact, doing with a mask what a hard light did here in the room. It is the reverse direction of [reading light](/reading-light): instead of finding the light that shapes the subject, you shape the light that is already in the file.

## Dodge and burn is this move, a century old

None of this began with sliders. In the wet darkroom, a print is made by shining the enlarger through the negative onto light-sensitive paper, and the longer a region is exposed the darker it prints. **Dodging** holds back some of that light, usually with a hand or a small card on a wire, so a chosen area receives less exposure and prints lighter. **Burning**, or burning-in, does the opposite, giving one region extra exposure through a hole in a card while the rest is shaded, so that area prints darker. Lighten here, darken there: the same two moves the mask makes, done with light and cardboard.

Ansel Adams, working out the Zone System with Fred Archer in the 1930s and 1940s, treated these as interpretation rather than repair. In his book *The Print* he put it directly: "I have often said that the negative is similar to a musician's score, and the print to the performance of that score. The negative comes to life only when 'performed' as a print." Dodging and burning were how he performed it, deciding at the enlarger where the eye should be led. The craft could be pushed astonishingly far. W. Eugene Smith was known to spend days on a single print, and one frame from his 1954 Albert Schweitzer essay for Life is reported to have taken him about five days at the enlarger to dodge and burn into its final form. When the terms carried into software, the tools changed but the idea did not. Photoshop still has literal Dodge and Burn brushes, retouchers build the same effect on a fifty-percent-grey layer in soft light, and in a raw editor you simply brush local exposure up or down. The mask is the modern dodging card, cut to shape by machine.

## Two cameras, two raw apps, one idea

The move survives whatever you develop in, which matters for a two-body kit. The Leica Q3 writes its raw as DNG, Adobe's open format, so Lightroom and Camera Raw read it with no conversion and the whole masking panel, AI selections and range masks included, is available to it directly. The Hasselblad X2D II writes 3FR, which is at home in Hasselblad's own Phocus. Phocus supports local corrections through adjustment layers, though a narrower set than Adobe's, centered on exposure, white balance, color, and moire, with tools like shadow fill, clarity, and highlight recovery added over time. Many X2D photographers simply take the files into Lightroom for the richer masking, which the format allows. Capture One, the other serious raw editor, runs every local edit through layers, each layer carrying its own mask drawn with the brush, a linear or radial gradient, or a luminance range, and it added AI subject and background masking of its own in the Capture One 23 release in October 2023.

The apps differ in how many selections they hand you and how smart the automatic ones are. They do not differ in the idea. A mask is a grayscale where, an adjustment is a what, and a local edit is the two multiplied. Learn the equation once and every editor is just a different set of tools for drawing the same shape.

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Watch the eye move">
Open a flat frame with a clear subject. Before you touch anything, notice where your eye goes first. Now select the subject and add a third of a stop of exposure, then lay a linear gradient across the sky or the brightest background and pull it down two thirds of a stop. Look again. The point is not the numbers, it is feeling the moment your attention stops wandering and settles on the subject, because you have just moved the peak of the frame's saliency onto it.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Build a mask by subtraction">
Take a portrait and run Select Subject, or Select People if you have it. Then hold the subtract modifier and brush the clothing out of the mask, so only the skin and face remain selected. Add a small exposure lift. You have made a compound mask, a where built from an AI selection minus a brush, and felt why the set operations matter: the fastest first cut is rarely the exact edge you want, and subtracting is how you refine it.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Range mask a sky the gradient cannot reach">
Find a frame where a ridgeline, a roof, or a tree breaks into the sky. Darken the sky first with a linear gradient and watch it dim the intruding shape along with it. Undo, and this time use a Luminance Range or Color Range keyed to the sky. The selection now skips the darker object and holds only the bright sky. Burn it down and compare. This is the difference between selecting by place and selecting by content.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="04" title="Dodge and burn a portrait by hand">
Ignore the automatic tools for one edit. Make one brush mask on the face and raise its exposure slightly, then a second brush mask on the background and lower its exposure. Keep both moves small, and stop while the effect is felt but not nameable. You are performing the print the way Adams described, leading the eye with two strokes of local tone, which is the whole of dodging and burning stripped to its two original gestures.
</ExerciseCard>
