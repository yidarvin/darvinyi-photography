A raw file you cannot find is worth almost as little as one you never shot, and a raw file you kept in exactly one place is one dropped drive away from gone. [The digital negative](/the-digital-negative) made the case for keeping the sensor's own measurement. This chapter is about the two unglamorous jobs that stand between pressing the shutter and developing that negative: getting it off the card without losing or corrupting it, and putting it somewhere you can find again in five years. Ingest is the copy. The catalog is the index. Every other editing chapter assumes both are already done, because they are the one part of the workflow where a mistake is permanent. A bad exposure you can reshoot or rescue. A frame that never got copied, or got copied wrong, or got a name that overwrote another frame, is simply not there anymore.

## The card is not a backup

Until you copy a frame off the card, it exists in exactly one place, and one place is not safe. Memory cards fail. Not often on a good card, but the probability is never zero and the cost is total, because the pictures on a dead card are usually gone for good. That is why cameras with two card slots let you write every frame twice at the moment of capture, and why the first thing to do after a shoot is get the frames onto a real drive, verified, before the card goes back in the camera and gets reformatted.

Verified is the word that carries the weight. When you drag files from the card to a drive in the Finder or in Explorer, the copy reports success the instant the write finishes. It does not read the files back to confirm that every byte arrived. Most of the time they did. Occasionally a bit flips, a file truncates, or the camera's folder structure comes across incompletely, and nothing warns you until you open the frame months later and find it broken. A verified copy closes that gap: it reads the written files back and compares their checksums against the source, so a bad copy is caught while the card is still in your hand. Dedicated offload tools do this by design. Hedge, now called OffShoot, verifies every transfer with a fast checksum and flags any copy whose bytes do not match the source; rsync will compare by content with its checksum option. It is worth knowing what the everyday tools do not do here. Lightroom Classic's import does not checksum-verify what it copies, and Photo Mechanic's Ingest gives you redundancy by writing to a primary and a secondary destination at once rather than a per-file checksum. Redundancy and verification are different safeguards, and a careful ingest wants both: copy to two places, and confirm the copy is sound.

## Three copies, two media, one offsite

Redundancy has a shape, and the shape has a name. The 3-2-1 rule was coined by the photographer Peter Krogh in *The DAM Book* and has since been adopted as general advice by everyone from US-CERT to the backup industry. It reads: keep three copies of any file you care about, one primary and two backups; keep them on two different types of media; and keep one of the copies offsite. Each number defeats a different way of losing the picture.

Three copies, because two beat a single drive failure and a third gives you a margin while you replace the one that died. Two media, because two copies on two drives of the same make and batch tend to fail from the same flaw at the same time, and a rewritable backup can inherit a deletion or a ransomware encryption straight from the copy it mirrors. Genuinely different media fail independently, which is the whole point of the number. One offsite, because a fire, a flood, or a burglary takes the entire room and every copy in it at once, and the only copy that survives is the one somewhere else. Cloud storage is convenient here for a plain reason: a single cloud copy is a second medium and an offsite copy in one.

The frame this protects hardest is the raw negative, because it is the one file you cannot regenerate. Every export, every edit, every version of the picture comes back out of it. ## Two layers: the negatives and the index

Once the frames are safely on disk, you point a cataloging application at them, and this is where most confusion about photo libraries begins. The catalog is not your photos. In Lightroom Classic the catalog is a single database file, and Adobe describes it plainly: for each photo it stores a reference to where the file lives on disk, the develop instructions you have applied, the metadata, and a preview. The original images never move into it. They stay in their folders, and the catalog only points at them.

Two consequences follow. Move or rename a file outside the application and the catalog loses track of it, so the frame shows up as missing or offline, its pointer now aimed at nothing. And deleting the catalog deletes no negatives. You would lose your edits and your organization, not your pictures. The files and the database are two separate layers, and the durable one is the files.

Capture One draws the same line as a choice you make up front. A Catalog behaves like Lightroom's database, while a Session keeps everything for one shoot inside a single self-contained folder, which many photographers prefer for exactly the independence it gives. Hasselblad's Phocus imports and organizes 3FR files in the same spirit. Whatever the tool, the model holds: the negatives are one thing, and the index built over them is another.

This layer above the files is also where your edits live. Developing a raw never rewrites the raw. The edits are kept as instructions, held either inside the catalog database or in a small XMP sidecar file that sits next to the original. Adobe writes that sidecar for proprietary raws like the X2D II's 3FR, and can embed the same information inside a DNG instead. XMP carries more than develop settings, too: your ratings, color labels, and keywords ride there as well. So the negative stays byte for byte what the camera recorded, while everything you decide about it, how it looks and how it is filed, lives in a layer you can back up on its own and carry from one program to the next.

The instruction that this layer stores is small and plain. A develop edit that would take a paragraph to describe is a short list of numbers on disk, and moving the negative to another program, or losing the catalog and rebuilding it, means carrying those numbers, not the pixels they act on.

## The cull

A shoot is mostly frames you will never use. The cull is the fast pass that separates the few worth developing from the many that are not, and doing it well is a discipline of speed rather than deliberation. The tools are simple and shared across programs: pick and reject flags for a quick keep-or-lose decision, one-to-five star ratings for grading the keepers, and color labels for grouping or marking a stage of work. Pick one system and hold to it, or the ratings stop meaning anything.

The professional habit is to decide quickly and not relitigate. A common method is two light passes. On the first, reject only the clear failures, the blinks and misfires and missed focus. On the second, flag the keepers, and pick the first frame that works in a burst instead of comparing ten near-identical frames for a winner. Photo Mechanic earned its reputation among event and sports photographers on exactly this speed, and the speed has a mechanism: it shows you the full-size JPEG preview the camera already embedded inside the raw rather than rendering each raw from scratch. The cull is what turns a folder of two thousand frames into a short list of picks you will actually return to.

## Findable years from now

The catalog earns its keep long after the shoot, on the day you need one specific frame out of tens of thousands. Two habits at ingest make that day easy, and both cost nothing at the time.

The first is a folder structure that owes nothing to any one application. A date-based tree, a folder per year holding a folder per day like 2026/2026-07-01, is the durable default. Peter Krogh calls these date buckets, and their virtue is that any operating system on any future machine can read them, catalog or no catalog. The files are the layer that has to survive a change of software, so give them a home the software did not invent. Rename the files as they land, as well, to a unique scheme built from the date and a running number. Cameras name files with a four-digit counter, and on many bodies that counter rolls over after 9999 and starts again, so a single camera will eventually hand two different frames the same name, something like DSC_0001. Some makers, Leica among them, fold a folder number into the name so that one body never repeats itself, but point two cameras of the same make at the same shoot and the names line up anyway. Unique names on import mean no frame can ever quietly overwrite another, whatever the camera called it.

The second habit is to lean on metadata. Every file already carries the technical facts of its own making, written automatically by the camera: the capture date and time, the body and the lens, the focal length, aperture, shutter speed, and ISO, and the location if the camera knew it. None of that took any tagging, and a catalog can search and filter on all of it, so finding every 90mm frame shot wide open on one particular morning is a matter of setting a filter. Keywords add what the camera cannot know: who is in the frame, the name of the place, the project it belongs to. The one rule with keywords is to add the terms you will actually search for and no more, because a tag you never query is work done for nothing, and the free metadata already answers most of your questions.

<Callout>
Ingest is a verified, redundant copy off the card; the catalog is the index that finds the frame and holds your edits without touching it. Copy every frame off the card before you reformat, verify the copy so a silent corruption cannot slip through, and keep three copies on two kinds of media with one offsite. Then point a catalog at the files, and remember that it only points: the negatives are one layer and the database over them is another, so a date-based folder tree and unique filenames are the part that actually has to last.
</Callout>

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Verify one ingest, then reformat">
The next card you offload, use a tool that checksums the copy instead of dragging the files across in the Finder. Watch it read the written files back and confirm each one matches. Only then reformat the card in the camera. You have just closed the single gap where a frame can vanish without a single warning.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Break your own backup on paper">
Write down every place a given raw currently lives. Now cross off everything inside your home and ask what is left. If the answer is nothing, you do not have a backup, you have several copies of the same accident. Add the missing offsite copy today. A cloud backup is the easiest way to satisfy the offsite and the second medium at the same time.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Move a file and watch the catalog break">
In Lightroom or Capture One, find a cataloged frame, then rename or move its file in the Finder, outside the application. Go back to the app and see the frame flagged missing. Reconnect it. That broken link is the whole mental model in one move: the catalog points at the file, it does not contain it, so the files are what you organize to last.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="04" title="Cull a shoot in two passes, on a clock">
Take a recent shoot and give yourself one quick pass to reject only the failures, then a second to flag the keepers, without comparing frames endlessly. Time it. Notice how much faster the decision goes when it is binary and you refuse to relitigate. The handful of picks that remain are exactly what the catalog exists to hand back to you later.
</ExerciseCard>
