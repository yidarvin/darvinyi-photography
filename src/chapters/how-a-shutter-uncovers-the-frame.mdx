Shutter speed answers one question, how long the sensor collects light, and [the last chapter](/shutter-speed-and-motion) was about that duration. The same control answers a second question more quietly: in what pattern does the shutter bare the sensor? Not every shutter uncovers the frame the same way. Some throw the whole frame open at a single instant. Others slide a narrow gap across it, so the top of the picture is recorded a few milliseconds before the bottom. That difference never shows up as brightness, but it decides two things you can see in the photograph. Whether a single flash lights the whole frame, and whether a fast subject keeps its shape or shears. Four mechanisms are in wide use, and one question sorts them all.

## One question sorts them

Ask of any shutter: at some instant, is the entire frame open at once? If the answer is yes, a single burst of flash can light all of it, and a subject that moves during the exposure is caught by every part of the frame at the same time, so its geometry stays true. If the answer is no, the frame is uncovered progressively, a moving window passing over it, and both of those guarantees fail. A flash catches only the strip the window happens to be crossing. A fast subject is recorded at slightly different moments up and down the frame.

Two of the four mechanisms answer yes at every speed, the leaf shutter and the global electronic shutter. Both open the whole frame together. The other two answer no once the exposure gets short, the focal-plane shutter above its sync speed and the rolling electronic shutter almost always. The figure lays the four side by side as space-time plots, sensor rows down the side and time across the bottom, with the light-collecting window shaded. The shape of that shaded region is the whole story. A vertical band means the frame is open all at once. A leaning slit means a window is traveling across it.

## The traveling slit: the focal-plane shutter

Most interchangeable-lens cameras, the Sony, Nikon, and Canon bodies with a lens mount, put the shutter right in front of the sensor. It is a pair of curtains, a first curtain that opens and a second curtain that closes, traveling across the frame one after the other, usually top to bottom. Set a slow or moderate speed and they take turns cleanly. The first curtain runs down and fully clears the sensor, the whole frame sits open for a while, then the second curtain runs down and covers it again. Every point on the sensor is exposed during the same window. The shutter-speed number is just the gap in time between the first curtain leaving and the second one arriving.

The catch is that a curtain is a physical object and takes real time to cross the frame, a few milliseconds, on the order of 1/250 of a second on a full-frame body. That travel time sets a ceiling. Once you ask for a shutter speed shorter than it, there is no longer enough time to fully open before you must start closing, so the second curtain launches while the first is still on its way. Now the two run down the frame together with a gap between them, and only that gap, a moving slit, is ever open. Each strip of sensor is bared just for the moment the slit passes over it. The exposure per point is genuinely short, which is the fast shutter speed you asked for, but at no instant is the whole frame open at once.

The fastest speed at which the frame is still fully open, the last speed before the slit forms, is the flash-sync speed, also written X-sync. On a modern full-frame camera it sits around 1/200 to 1/250, with some bodies reaching 1/320. The Sony a7 IV syncs at 1/250. That number is not a marketing choice, it is the curtain's travel time wearing a different label. It is the mechanism behind the dark band you get when a flash fires too fast, and behind high-speed sync, both of which [flash sync and the leaf advantage](/flash-sync-and-the-leaf-advantage) takes up.

One common variation is worth naming. Many mirrorless cameras offer an electronic first-curtain shutter, or EFCS. An electronic reset of the sensor starts the exposure in place of the mechanical first curtain, and the mechanical second curtain still closes to end it. The point is to remove the small jolt of the first curtain snapping open, the shutter shock that can smear fine detail on a tripod. The cost shows up only at the fast end with a wide aperture: the mechanical second curtain travels a few millimeters in front of the sensor plane, so at fast speeds its edge shaves the cone of out-of-focus light and cuts bokeh discs flat, which is why cameras cap EFCS and hand off to the full mechanical shutter above a set speed. Neither camera in this book uses a focal-plane shutter, but almost every other camera you pick up will, so its sync ceiling is worth carrying.

## The iris in the lens: the leaf shutter

Move the shutter off the sensor and into the lens, and the whole picture changes. A leaf shutter is a ring of thin overlapping blades sitting right at the aperture, inside the lens. To make an exposure the blades spring open from the center outward, uncovering the whole opening at once, hold for the set time, then close back the same way. There is no slit and nothing travels across the frame. Light reaches every part of the sensor over the same interval, at every shutter speed. That is the defining property, and its most useful consequence is that a leaf shutter syncs a flash at any speed. Both cameras in this book use one. The Leica Q3's shutter lives inside its fixed 28mm lens, and every Hasselblad XCD lens carries its own leaf shutter, which is why the X2D II has no shutter in the body at all.

Nothing is free. Because the blades have mass and must fully open and then reverse, a leaf shutter tops out slower than a focal-plane one. Conventional leaf shutters stopped around 1/500. Modern electronically timed ones do better: the Q3 reaches 1/2000, and the newest Hasselblad XCD lenses reach 1/4000, with older ones at 1/2000. There is also a subtler cost, called shutter efficiency. At the fastest speeds the blades spend a real fraction of the exposure still opening or already closing, so they never quite sit fully open, and less light lands on the sensor than a fully-open shutter of the same marked time would pass. Efficiency runs somewhere around 60 to 90 percent, and it is worse wide open, where the blades have farther to travel, than stopped down. It is a small effect, but it is why a leaf shutter's fastest mechanical speeds run a little thin on light.

## No moving parts: the electronic shutter

The sensor can also time the exposure itself, with no shutter at all. In electronic-shutter mode each row of pixels is reset to begin collecting light and later read out to stop, and the interval between the two is the shutter speed. The reset and the readout each march down the sensor row by row, so an exposure wave sweeps from top to bottom. Every row gets the same duration, but each one starts and ends a little later than the row above it. This is a rolling shutter, and it is the same leaning-slit pattern as a fast focal-plane shutter, drawn in electronics instead of curtains.

What matters is how long that wave takes to cross the frame, the readout time, first row to last. On a conventional full-frame sensor it is long, tens of milliseconds, about 62 on the Sony a7 III and about 51 on the Nikon Z6. On a stacked sensor, with its readout circuitry moved onto a layer behind the pixels, it drops to a few milliseconds, about 3.7 on the Nikon Z9 and 3.9 on the Sony a1.

The electronic shutter buys real advantages. It is silent, it has no shutter shock, and because nothing physical has to accelerate it reaches speeds a mechanical shutter cannot, 1/16000 on the Q3 and 1/6000 on the X2D II, which is how you freeze motion in bright light once the leaf shutter has run out of speed. The price is the progressive uncovering. A subject that moves during the readout is recorded at shifting positions and shears, so straight lines lean. Light that flickers, which is most LED and fluorescent lighting, paints horizontal bands across the frame because different rows catch it at different points in its cycle. A single flash burst mostly will not sync, for the same reason it fails past a focal-plane camera's sync speed. [Rolling shutter and readout](/rolling-shutter-and-readout), the next chapter, takes that distortion apart in detail.

## All at once, in electronics: the global shutter

There is a fourth mechanism that takes the strengths of the other electronic shutter without its weakness, and it is still rare. A global shutter is electronic, with no moving parts, but every pixel starts and stops its exposure at the same instant. Each pixel holds its charge in a small storage node beside it, so the whole frame can be frozen together and read out afterward without a wave. It is a leaf shutter's whole-frame-at-once behavior with none of the mechanics: zero rolling distortion, and flash sync at any speed.

The reason it is not everywhere is cost, in money and in image quality both. The extra storage circuitry at every pixel takes up room that would otherwise collect light, so a global-shutter sensor tends to carry a higher base ISO and less dynamic range than a comparable rolling sensor. The first full-frame camera to ship with a global shutter was the Sony a9 III, announced late in 2023. It syncs flash at every speed up to a top of 1/80000 and shoots 120 frames a second with no distortion at all, and it pays for that with a base ISO of 250, about a stop and a third above the ISO 100 of its peers, and correspondingly less highlight room. [ISO, the sensor, and dynamic range](/iso-sensor-and-dynamic-range) explains why a higher base ISO costs you at the top of the tonal range. For now the global shutter is the electronic version of the leaf shutter's promise, arriving at a real price.

Sort the four by the one question and the pairs fall out cleanly. Leaf and global open the whole frame at once, so they sync a flash at any speed and never shear a moving subject. Focal-plane above its sync speed and rolling electronic slide a window across the frame, so their flash is limited and fast motion can distort. Both cameras in this book are leaf, which is a deliberate choice. It is what lets them drop a flash into bright sun at 1/2000, and it is why their electronic shutters, useful as they are for reaching 1/16000, come with the rolling-shutter caveats from the section above.

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Make the readout visible">
On either camera, switch from the mechanical leaf shutter to the electronic shutter and photograph something with a straight vertical edge moving fast across the frame: a passing car, a swinging pole, or pan the camera quickly past a building. On the leaf shutter the verticals stay upright. On the electronic shutter they lean, because the bottom of the frame was read a few milliseconds after the top. That lean is the exposure wave you cannot otherwise see.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Catch the flicker bands">
Point the camera at an LED screen or a bank of fluorescent tubes and shoot at a fast speed, first on the electronic shutter, then on the mechanical leaf shutter. The electronic frame is likely to show horizontal bands of brightness or color. The mechanical frame mostly will not. The bands are proof that the rows were exposed at different instants, each catching the light at a different point in its flicker.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Find the leaf shutter's ceiling">
In bright light with the aperture wide open, run the mechanical shutter up to its fastest speed. On the Q3 it stops at 1/2000, on an XCD lens at 1/2000 or 1/4000. To go any faster you have to switch to the electronic shutter. That wall is the blades reaching the limit of how fast they can open and close, the same mass that also lets them light a flash at every speed below it.
</ExerciseCard>
