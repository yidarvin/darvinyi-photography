The aperture is the hole the light comes through, and it does two jobs at once. It meters, by setting how much light reaches the sensor, and it composes, by setting how much of the scene comes out sharp. You met the first job in [the exposure triangle](/the-exposure-triangle). The second is the reason photographers choose a lens by its widest aperture and pay dearly for the last stop of it. This chapter is about that second job, depth of field, and about a fact the f-number quietly hides: the same f-number is a different hole on a different lens, and a different depth of field on a different format. f/1.7 on the Leica Q3 is not f/1.7 on the Hasselblad X2D, and by the end you will know exactly why, and by how much.

## The plane and the zone

Focus lands on a plane. A lens renders exactly one distance in true focus, a flat plane parallel to the sensor, and everything nearer or farther is technically a blur. What saves the photograph is that a small enough blur still reads as a point. Depth of field is the band of distances, some in front of the plane of focus and more behind it, where the blur stays small enough to pass as sharp.

"Small enough" has a name and a number. The circle of confusion is the largest a point may smear on the sensor and still look like a point in the finished image. For full frame the standard value is about 0.03mm, which comes from a rule of thumb worth knowing: the sensor's diagonal divided by 1500. Because that number is tied to how much the capture has to be enlarged to reach a print, it scales with the format. A smaller sensor gets enlarged more, so it demands a tighter circle; a larger sensor tolerates a looser one. Hold that thought, because it returns the moment the two formats meet.

The band is not centered on the subject. It always runs deeper behind the plane of focus than in front, and the imbalance grows with distance. The familiar rule that a third of the depth sits in front and two thirds behind is a fair guess at ordinary distances and simply wrong at the edges: up close the split is nearly even, and once you focus at the hyperfocal distance the far limit runs clean to infinity. That distance, where everything from half of it to infinity holds sharp, is about f squared over N times c, the focal length squared divided by the f-number times the circle of confusion. It is the landscape photographer's number, and Part IV leans on it.

## The size of the hole

Here is the pivot the chapter turns on. The f-number is not a size. It is a ratio, the focal length divided by the diameter of the opening. f/2 on a 50mm lens is a 25mm hole; f/2 on a 100mm lens is a 50mm hole. Same f-number, same exposure, very different holes.

Depth of field follows the hole, not the ratio. Hold the framing fixed, so the subject fills the same part of the frame, and the depth of field is governed by the physical diameter of that opening, the entry pupil. Cambridge in Colour shows the point cleanly: at a constant subject size, lenses from 10mm to 400mm give nearly identical depth of field at the same f-number, because matching the framing cancels the focal length and leaves only the aperture diameter. The wider the actual hole, the shallower the zone, and the larger a point in the background smears. That background blur is directly proportional to the diameter. Double the hole and you double the size of the blur disc.

This is why a fast lens earns its bulk. The Q3's 28mm f/1.7 opens a hole about 16mm across, and that physical opening, not the number engraved on the barrel, is what lifts a subject off its background.

## Why f/1.7 is not f/1.7

Now mount the same lens speed on two different sensors and the ratio stops being portable. A larger sensor takes in a wider angle from any given lens, so to frame the subject the same way you reach for a longer focal length, or you step closer. Either way the magnification climbs. And a longer focal length at the same f-number, by the definition above, opens a wider hole. The bigger format shoots through a bigger opening for the same f-number, so it renders shallower and blurs the background more.

There is a clean way to put a number on it. Multiply the f-number by the crop factor, the ratio of the sensor diagonals, and you get the equivalent aperture: the full-frame f-number that would give the same depth of field and gather the same total light. The Hasselblad's 44 by 33 sensor, actually 43.8 by 32.9mm, has a diagonal near 55mm against full frame's 43mm, a crop factor of 0.79. So every f-number on the X2D is worth about a fifth of a stop more depth-of-field separation than the same number on the Q3.

Run the thesis through it. f/1.7 on the Q3 is f/1.7. f/1.7 on the X2D, if such a lens existed, would render like f/1.3 on full frame, shallower than any native full-frame f/1.7 lens can reach. The figure holds the framing and the f-number fixed and lets only the format change.

In practice Hasselblad does not sell an f/1.7 prime. The fast XCD primes stop at f/2.5, with one older f/1.9. On paper that sounds slow beside the Q3's f/1.7. It is not. f/2.5 on the X2D renders depth of field like f/2.0 on full frame and gathers the total light of an f/2.0 lens, so the "slow" medium-format prime lands in the same shallow territory as a fast full-frame one. The number on the barrel undersells it by a fifth of a stop of format.

One caution keeps the model from overreaching. Equivalent aperture governs depth of field and total light, the light gathered over the whole frame, which the sensor chapter will tie to noise. It does not govern exposure. f/2.5 meters as f/2.5 on any format; the medium-format sensor never needs a brighter exposure, it simply collects more light because it is larger. Keep exposure and depth of field on separate ledgers and the equivalence stops being confusing.

<Callout>
The Q3's crop modes, its 35, 50, 75, and 90mm settings, reframe by cropping the 28mm lens, not by changing it. The hole stays about 16mm wide, so a "75mm" crop frames like a 75mm but keeps the deep depth of field of a 28mm. To actually thin the depth of field you must open the aperture or move closer. Cropping only tightens the view.
</Callout>

## The floor on stopping down

Stopping down is the other direction, and it has a floor. A small aperture deepens the zone until nearly everything is sharp, which is exactly what a landscape wants. But past a point the light bends around the rim of the shrinking hole and the entire frame softens. This is diffraction, and it caps how far stopping down keeps paying.

Where the floor sits depends on the pixels. Both of your cameras carry very small photosites, about 3.8 thousandths of a millimeter, because the Q3 packs 60 megapixels onto full frame and the X2D packs 100 onto medium format. On sensors that fine the softening shows at the pixel level as early as f/5.6 to f/8, where a 24-megapixel body would not reveal it until around f/11. This does not put small apertures off limits. It means the sharpest aperture sits wider than you might guess, usually two or three stops down from wide open, and pushing on to f/16 spends some crispness to buy depth. Stop down for the depth when the picture needs it, and know you are paying for it in sharpness.

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Find your widest hole">
Take your fastest lens, focus on a subject about a meter away with a distant background, and shoot it wide open, then two stops down. Compare how far the background dissolves in each. Work out the entry pupil for both frames, the focal length divided by the f-number, so you can connect the amount of blur to the size of the physical hole rather than to the number on the dial.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Walk the format equivalence">
If you shoot both the Q3 and the X2D, frame the same portrait on each. To match the Q3's f/1.7 depth of field on the X2D, divide by the crop factor: 1.7 divided by 0.79 is about f/2.2. Shoot the Q3 at f/1.7 and the X2D at f/2.2 and compare the backgrounds. They should sit close, which is the crop factor proving itself. With one camera, run the same test between full frame and its crop mode.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Find the diffraction floor">
Photograph a flat, detailed subject, a brick wall or a printed page, square on, at every full stop from wide open to the minimum aperture. View the files at 100 percent. The frame sharpens for the first few stops, peaks, then softens as you keep closing down. The peak is your lens's sweet spot; the softening past it is diffraction, and now you know where your lens crosses it.
</ExerciseCard>
