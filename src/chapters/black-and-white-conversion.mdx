Converting to black and white is a decision, and the button most people press makes it for them, badly. Pull the saturation to zero and the converter falls back on one fixed recipe, the same weighted average of the channels for every frame, so colors that happened to reflect the same amount of light collapse into the same grey. That is the value collapse from [seeing in mono](/black-and-white-seeing), and it is exactly what a real conversion exists to prevent. A good black-and-white file is three decisions stacked on a color one: which grey each color becomes, what the surface is made of, and whether any color comes back at the two ends of the scale. The mix, the grain, the split tone. All three ride on the [raw file](/the-digital-negative), which kept every color channel the sensor read, so the conversion is never baked in and never final. The last chapter taught you to see the grey before the shutter. This one is where you build it, on the full-color negative the camera actually recorded.

## The desaturate button decides nothing

Start with what the automatic conversion does, because the whole chapter is a reaction to it. Desaturating keeps a single number for each pixel, the luminance, close to a fixed 0.21 of the red, 0.72 of the green, and 0.07 of the blue. Those weights match how the eye is built, green bright and blue faint, but they are the same for every picture, so the converter never asks what this particular frame needed. A red barn and the green grass beside it can carry almost the same luminance, and when they do, the automatic grey renders them as one tone and the barn dissolves into the field.

Setting a photo's Treatment to Black & White in Lightroom does something better from the first click. It applies a monochrome profile, usually Adobe Monochrome, and opens the Black & White Mix, the panel that lets you overrule those fixed weights color by color. The automatic mix becomes a starting point, not a verdict, and the three real decisions open up in front of you.

## The mix: the colored filter, now eight sliders

The mix answers the first and largest question, which grey each color becomes. In Lightroom the Black & White Mix splits the spectrum into the same eight bands as the [color mixer](/color-grading), red, orange, yellow, green, aqua, blue, purple, and magenta, but with only one control each. Hue and saturation are gone, because the color is gone, so all that is left is brightness. Drag the blue slider down and every blue in the frame, the sky most of all, darkens toward black. Drag the red slider up and a red barn lifts toward white. An Auto button will guess a balanced mix to begin from.

This is the glass contrast filter of the film era, rebuilt as sliders. [Seeing in mono](/black-and-white-seeing) promised as much: a red filter over the lens lightened reds and darkened cyans because it passed its own color and blocked the opposite, and it cost a stop or two of light and could never be undone. The mix does the same job to the tones, for free, after the fact, and reversibly, because the [raw file](/the-digital-negative) still holds all three channels. The blue slider is the sky-darkening red filter. The whole ladder of yellow, orange, and red filters is now a set of numbers you dial to taste and change your mind about tomorrow.

The other editors phrase the same idea in different words. Photoshop has two tools for it. The older Channel Mixer, with Monochrome checked, builds the grey from percentages of the red, green, and blue channels, defaulting to 40, 40, and 20, and Adobe's own advice is to keep the three totalling about 100 so the picture neither brightens nor crushes its highlights as you rebalance them. The dedicated Black & White adjustment, added in Photoshop CS3 in 2007, is friendlier: six color sliders, Reds, Yellows, Greens, Cyans, Blues, and Magentas, plus an on-image tool you drag right on the sky to darken every blue at once. Capture One does it through the Color Sensitivity sliders in its Black & White tool, negative to darken a color and positive to lighten it. Different panels, one idea. You are choosing, band by band, how color turns into tone.

One caution before you push a slider to its stop. The mix is strong enough to force a separation the scene never had, and a heavy push on a broad, smooth area like a blue sky can pull it so far that the gentle gradient across it breaks into visible steps, the posterization raw editors call banding. That is the cue for the second decision.

## Grain: the surface the tones sit on

Grain is the surface the tones sit on, and adding it back is a choice with several good reasons. The obvious one is the look. Real film scattered its image across countless tiny clumps of metallic silver, and a picture built from those clumps reads as film in a way a perfectly smooth digital file does not. The practical reasons matter more still. A little grain is dither: sprinkled across the banded sky from the last section, it breaks the hard steps back into a smooth-looking gradient, because the eye averages the speckle. It can disguise digital noise, trading the blotchy, colored mess of a pushed high-ISO frame for an even, film-like texture the eye forgives. And a consistent grain laid over a whole series ties the frames into one body of work.

Lightroom builds it from three sliders in the Effects panel. Amount sets how much, Size sets how coarse each speck is, and Roughness sets how even or irregular the speckle looks, from a clean, regular pattern to a rough, organic one. Past a Size of about 25 the tool quietly mixes in a little blue so the grain sits better next to noise reduction.

It helps to know what you are imitating. Film grain is the clumped silver left after development, and its size is set by the emulsion. Faster film needs larger silver-halide crystals to catch enough light, and larger crystals leave coarser, more visible grain, which is why a box of ISO 400 film looks grittier than ISO 100. Kodak measured it: Tri-X 400, the classic reportage stock, carries an RMS granularity of 17, which its datasheet still calls fine, and pushing it two or three stops for a dim room grows the grain along with the contrast. That number is the objective side, the granularity. Graininess is the subjective impression you actually see in the print.

The one thing to hold onto in practice is that grain has a real size and the picture does not. A speck sized to look right on a large print is a fraction of a percent of the frame, and shrink that same frame to fit a phone screen and the speck shrinks with it, until the grain either vanishes or turns to mush. Set grain for the size you will actually deliver, and judge it at that size, not at a convenient zoom.

## Split-tone: color back in at the two ends

The last decision lets color back in, under control. In the darkroom this was toning, a chemical bath after development that replaced the print's metallic silver with something more stable and a different color. Sepia converted the silver to silver sulfide and turned the print a warm brown, and because silver sulfide resists the air better than bare silver, the print also lasted longer. Selenium converted it to silver selenide, deepened the blacks, and shifted the shadows toward a cool, purplish aubergine, while adding the same archival permanence. Gold gave a cool blue-black and protected the print further. Iron toning made a Prussian blue, though it bought no permanence and sometimes cost a little. Toning was never only cosmetic. It changed what the print was made of.

Split toning is the refinement: one color in the shadows and a different color in the highlights, rather than a single wash over everything. That is the whole definition, and it is the same instrument you met in [color grading](/color-grading). Adobe's old Split Toning panel, a hue and a saturation for the highlights, the same for the shadows, and a balance between them, was folded into the Color Grading wheels in 2020, which only added midtones and a global tint on top of it. On a grey image those wheels do exactly what a pair of toning baths did, and the tasteful version is the monochrome cousin of teal and orange: a low, cool blue in the shadows and a low, warm amber in the highlights, kept quiet enough to read as a toned print rather than a color one. Your Q3 already ships this. The Selenium [Leica Look](/color-grading) is a canned split tone, cool shadows and a faint warmth, applied in the camera. Capture One keeps a Split Tones tab inside its black-and-white tool for the same purpose.

One shaping move sits under all three and is easy to forget: the contrast of the print itself. The mix set which grey each color became, but how hard those greys separate is the slope of the tone curve, the same S from [tone and the curve](/tone-and-the-curve). A black-and-white print usually wants more of it than the flat file gives, so a common last move is to deepen the blacks, lift the whites, and steepen the midtones until the picture reads with weight.

## Two cameras, and the sensor that throws color away

All three decisions depend on one fact: the color is still in the file. A raw capture keeps every channel the sensor read, so the conversion is a set of instructions applied at development, not a change written into the pixels, and you can redo it forever. That is the standing promise of [the digital negative](/the-digital-negative), and black and white is where it pays off most, because the same negative can become a dozen different monochrome prints and none of them spends the original.

Both of your bodies keep the color. The Leica Q3 has a normal color sensor, and you can set a monochrome Leica Look so the finder and rear screen show the scene in grey while the DNG quietly records every channel, which lets you previsualize in black and white and still convert from full color later. The Hasselblad X2D II shows no monochrome preview at all, so there the grey stays in your head until you open the 3FR in Phocus or Lightroom. Either way the conversion happens on a color file.

The instructive exception is a camera built to give all of this up. Leica's Monochrom bodies, the Q2 Monochrom, the M11 Monochrom, and the Q3 Monochrom that arrived in late 2025, remove the color filter array from the sensor so every photosite records only brightness. There is no color to remix, so there is no channel mix and no digital filter, only real glass filters over the lens as in the film days, and the black and white is committed the instant you press the shutter. What you get in return is roughly a stop of added sensitivity and visibly more resolution, because the sensor no longer interpolates color across neighboring pixels. It is the film bargain again, made of silicon: commit at capture, and be paid for it. The color bodies take the other side of the deal, keeping the color and keeping the decision open.

Whatever you convert in, the tools converge on the same three moves. Nik Silver Efex Pro, now DxO's, folds them into one panel, with film-stock emulations, a grain engine that re-renders the picture rather than dusting noise on top, a Structure slider for micro-contrast, and control points for local work. DxO FilmPack and Capture One's black-and-white tool do the same job in their own arrangement. Learn the three decisions once, the mix, the grain, and the tone, and each of these is only a different room for making them.

<Callout>
A black-and-white conversion is a decision, not a button, and the automatic desaturate is the one version that decides nothing, collapsing colors of equal brightness into the same grey. A real conversion is three choices stacked on a color raw. The mix decides which grey each color becomes, the same eight bands as the color mixer reduced to brightness, which is the film era's glass contrast filter rebuilt as free, reversible sliders. Grain decides the surface, imitating the clumped silver of fast film, dithering away banding and disguising noise, sized for the output you will actually deliver. The split tone decides whether color returns at the ends, a cool shadow and a warm highlight in the tradition of selenium and sepia, made with the same wheels as a color grade. All of it rides on the raw file keeping every channel, which is exactly what a true monochrome sensor gives up in exchange for resolution and speed. Keep the color, and the black-and-white print stays a decision you can remake forever.
</Callout>

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Beat the automatic grey">
Photograph a colored subject against a background of a different but similarly bright hue: a red door in a green hedge, a blue boat on grey water. Convert it with a plain desaturate and watch the subject merge into the ground. Now open the black-and-white mix and drag the subject's color one way and the background's the other, until the two tones separate. You have done by hand the one thing the fixed weights could never do for you, and you can feel the difference between a conversion that decides and one that does not.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Rebuild the red filter">
Find a frame with a blue sky and white clouds. In the mix, pull the blue band down and watch the sky darken while the clouds, which are neutral, stand off it untouched. That is the red-filter look from the last chapter, made after the shutter and free to undo. Now keep pushing until the sky breaks into visible bands, then ease back to just before it does. You have found both the tool and its limit in a single move, and the limit is where grain comes in.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Grain for two sizes">
Take one converted frame and add grain until it looks right at full size on your screen. Export it twice, once small for the web and once at print dimensions, and look at each at its real size, not zoomed. The grain that was perfect at one size is wrong at the other, too coarse in the print or gone entirely on the web. Set the grain for where the picture will actually be seen, and check it there.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="04" title="Split a toned print">
On a black-and-white frame with clear shadows and highlights, open the Color Grading wheels and put a low, cool blue in the shadows and a low, warm amber in the highlights. Keep the saturation small, so it reads as a toned print and not a color cast. Compare it against the plain grey version, and against the Selenium Leica Look if you shoot the Q3, and decide which the picture wanted. You are choosing a mood, not fixing a problem, which is the whole difference between toning and correcting.
</ExerciseCard>

- Adobe, [Introducing Color Grading](https://blog.adobe.com/en/publish/2020/10/20/introducing-color-grading), for the October 2020 replacement of the two-zone Split Toning panel (Highlights and Shadows hue and saturation, plus Balance) by the Color Grading wheels, which add Midtones and a Global wheel and per-range luminance while remaining fully compatible with the old Split Toning; with Northlandscapes, [What is split toning](https://www.northlandscapes.com/articles/what-is-split-toning-and-how-to-use-it-in-lightroom), for the classic low-saturation cool-shadow, warm-highlight recipe. The Q3's named Selenium creative style is covered under [Leica Looks](https://leica-camera.com/en-int/photography/leica-looks).
- [Wikipedia, Nik Collection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nik_Collection) and [Nik Software](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nik_Software), for Silver Efex Pro's lineage (Nik Software, acquired by Google in 2012, released free in 2016, sold to DxO in 2017 and updated since); with Digital Photography School, [8 reasons to use Silver Efex Pro 2](https://digital-photography-school.com/8-reasons-to-use-silver-efex-pro-2-for-your-black-and-white-conversions/) and [DxO, Nik Silver Efex](https://nikcollection.dxo.com/nik-silver-efex/), for the film-stock emulations, the re-rendering grain engine, the Structure micro-contrast slider, and U Point control points. [DxO FilmPack](https://www.dxo.com/dxo-filmpack/) is the film-emulation alternative.
- [DPReview, Leica Q3 Monochrom review](https://www.dpreview.com/reviews/leica-q3-monochrom-review) and [PetaPixel, The Leica Q3 Monochrom has a dedicated black-and-white 60MP sensor](https://petapixel.com/2025/11/20/the-leica-q3-monochrom-has-a-dedicated-black-and-white-60mp-sensor/), for the color Q3 shooting a monochrome JPEG preview while keeping a full-color DNG, and for the Monochrom bodies (Q2 Monochrom 2020, M11 Monochrom 2023, Q3 Monochrom announced November 2025) omitting the color filter array to record luminance only, trading any color remix for about a stop of sensitivity and higher resolution with no demosaic. Hasselblad's post-only black-and-white workflow (no in-camera monochrome preview; conversion in Phocus, and 3FR into Lightroom or Capture One) is per [Hasselblad, X2D II 100C FAQ](https://www.hasselblad.com/x-system/x2d-ii-100c-faq/).
