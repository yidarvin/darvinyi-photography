Landscape and architecture are the deliberate end of photography, the opposite of the [street](/street-and-documentary). Nothing in front of you is going anywhere. The mountain will hold its pose for the rest of your life, the building longer than that. What you give up in urgency you get back in control, and the whole craft shifts because of it. On the street you settle everything you can in advance so the only thing left is the moment. Here there is no moment to catch, so you spend your attention on the frame itself: on holding every fine detail from the near rocks to the far ridge, on keeping the whole range of light from the shadowed valley to the bright sky, and on keeping the lines that are straight in the world straight in the picture. This is the chapter the Hasselblad X2D II was built for, and it is the most literal reading of this book's title. When nothing rushes you, every deliberate choice shows.

## The still subject

The defining fact of landscape and architecture is time. [Street work](/street-and-documentary) is reaction under a stopwatch. This is the reverse. You can put the camera on a tripod, wait for the light to arrive rather than chase it, and make the technically best frame the situation allows instead of the fastest one. Almost every technique in this chapter depends on that stillness. A tripod at base ISO for the cleanest file, an aperture chosen for sharpness rather than speed, several frames blended for depth or for range, verticals leveled with care. None of it is available to someone working handheld in a hurry. The reward for slowing down is a frame that holds far more than a single quick exposure ever could.

## A frame that holds everything

The X2D II records 100 megapixels, an image 11,656 pixels wide by 8,742 tall, on a 44 by 33 millimeter sensor. That resolution is not about pixel-peeping. It is what lets a print hold up at wall size, and what gives you room to crop into a scene and still keep a full file. But resolution only becomes real detail if the whole frame is genuinely sharp, and at 100 megapixels the demands are strict. Two things fight you.

The first is diffraction. As [lenses and optics](/lenses-optics-and-perspective) explained, stopping down narrows the aperture, and past a point the light bends around the blades enough to soften every pixel. On this sensor, with its 3.76 micron photosites, the sharpest apertures sit around f/5.6 to f/8. Softening from diffraction is already measurable by f/8 and clearly limits the image by f/11, and by f/16 to f/22 the whole frame is diffraction-bound. The instinct to stop all the way down for more depth of field backfires: you trade the depth you wanted for a softness that no longer resolves the sensor.

The second is focus. A single frame set to the [hyperfocal distance](/street-and-documentary) puts everything from about half that distance to infinity into acceptable sharpness, which is enough for most work. But acceptable is a circle-of-confusion standard, the blur size a print forgives, and a 100-megapixel sensor out-resolves it, so a single hyperfocal frame cannot be truly sharp everywhere at the pixel level. When you want corner-to-corner critical sharpness, the answer is focus stacking. The X2D II will bracket focus for you as a drive mode, capturing a run of frames stepped through the scene that you blend later into one fully sharp image. The still subject is what makes this possible, because nothing shifts between frames.

## Holding the whole range of light

Landscapes are high-contrast scenes. The sky is often several stops brighter than the land beneath it, and the good light near dawn and dusk makes the gap wider still. Dynamic range is how much of that span a single file can hold, and it is the second thing medium format buys you.

Hasselblad quotes 15.3 stops for the X2D II. Independent measurement is stricter: Bill Claff's photographic dynamic range, which uses a tighter, print-referenced noise floor, puts it near 12.5 stops. Both numbers are honest. They measure different things, and the gap between a maker's engineering figure and a measured photographic one is normal, the same distinction [ISO and dynamic range](/iso-sensor-and-dynamic-range) drew earlier in the book. Either way, at base ISO the 16-bit file holds an enormous range, and the larger sensor gathers about two-thirds of a stop more light than full frame at the same exposure, which is where its cleaner shadows come from.

The working method follows from the [digital negative](/the-digital-negative). Expose to protect the highlights, because a blown sky is gone and cannot be brought back, then lift the shadows from the raw where the file has depth to spare, exactly as [tone and the curve](/tone-and-the-curve) laid out. When even twelve stops is not enough, the still subject rescues you again: bracket several exposures and blend them, or hold the sky down with a graduated neutral-density filter over the lens. And because both your cameras carry [leaf shutters](/flash-sync-and-the-leaf-advantage) that stay open for minutes at a time, a strong ND filter turns moving water to mist and drifting cloud to streaks, the long-exposure look that only a static camera on a tripod can make.

## Keeping the lines true

Architecture adds a demand landscapes rarely make: the lines have to stay honest. A building's vertical edges are parallel to each other, and the eye expects them parallel in the photograph. The moment they lean in toward the top, the picture looks like it is falling over.

The lean has one cause, and it is worth stating exactly because the fix follows straight from it. A building's sides stay parallel in the image only while the sensor plane is kept parallel to the building, which means keeping the camera back vertical and the lens pointed level at the horizon. Tilt the camera up to fit a tall building into the frame and the sensor plane is no longer parallel to the wall, so lines that are parallel in the world converge in the picture. Photographers call it keystoning, and the steeper you tilt, the harder they meet.

That leaves three honest ways to fit a tall building without tipping the camera. You can keep it level and accept that the top is cut off, which holds the lines true but loses the building. You can raise the lens instead of the camera, the movement a view camera calls rise and a tilt-shift lens calls shift: the lens projects a larger image circle than the sensor needs, so sliding it upward captures the top of the building while the sensor stays vertical and the verticals stay parallel. Or you can keep the camera level, frame wide with room above the roofline, and crop down to the building afterward. Hasselblad makes no shift lens for the X system, and its HTS tilt-shift adapter belongs to the older H system, so on the X2D that last method, or a third-party shift lens, is the practical route. The 100-megapixel file is what makes cropping viable, and it is the reason resolution and geometry turn out to be the same subject.

## Fixing it after, and what it costs

You can also correct the lean in software, and it pays to understand what that correction actually does. Lightroom's vertical transform and Hasselblad's Phocus both stretch the converging image back into a rectangle, pulling the narrow top out until the sides run parallel again. But stretching the top wider pushes its corners past the edges of the frame, so the software has to crop the picture back to a clean rectangle. The correction is a crop, and a crop spends resolution. This is the one place in the chapter where two of its subjects collide: the keystone is a geometry problem, and the fix is a resolution cost paid from the 100-megapixel budget.

The trade is real, and it is why a shift lens still exists in the age of software. Moving the lens keeps the full frame and the full resolution, while correcting afterward always gives some of both away. On a 100-megapixel file you can afford a heavy correction and still print big. On a 24-megapixel body the same correction can cost more than you have. Getting the verticals close to right in the camera, level and framed with room to spare, is what keeps the crop small and the file large.

## The deliberate photograph

This is the last chapter, and it is a fitting place to end, because landscape and architecture are where every idea in this book compounds. The [exposure triangle](/the-exposure-triangle), the [sensor's range](/iso-sensor-and-dynamic-range), the [lens's sweet spot](/lenses-optics-and-perspective), the [order of the edit](/order-of-operations), the [print at the end](/output-and-print): none of them is rushed here, so all of them show. A landscape shot at base ISO on a tripod, focus-stacked and exposed to hold the sky, with its verticals kept true, is a photograph in which nothing was left to chance. That is what the title has meant all along. A deliberate photograph is not a lucky one. It is one where you understood every choice the camera offered and made each of them on purpose.

<Callout>
Landscape and architecture are the deliberate opposite of the street: the subject holds still, so the work is making a single frame hold everything it can. Resolution gives detail and cropping room, but only if the frame is truly sharp, which means the f/5.6 to f/8 sweet spot rather than f/22, and focus stacking rather than one hyperfocal guess. Dynamic range holds the span from shadowed land to bright sky, so expose to protect the highlights and lift the shadows from the raw. And keeping lines true is a single rule: verticals stay parallel only while the sensor plane stays parallel to the building. Level the camera and shift, or crop from a wide level frame, and let the correction stretch and crop only as far as your resolution can pay. Everything slows down here, which is exactly why every deliberate choice shows.
</Callout>

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Level, then crop">
Shoot a tall building twice. Once tilted up to fit it into the frame, and once with the camera held dead level and framed with empty room above the roofline. Straighten the first in software, and crop the second down to the building. Compare them at full size. The leveled-and-cropped frame will be squarer and cleaner, and you will see exactly what the tilt cost you when you corrected it.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Find the sweet spot">
Put the camera on a tripod facing a scene full of fine texture: a field, a brick wall, a distant treeline. Shoot the same frame at f/5.6, f/8, f/11, f/16, and f/22 without moving anything else. View them at 100 percent. Somewhere near f/8 will be the sharpest, and f/22 will look soft across the whole frame. That softness is diffraction, and seeing it once is what stops you from reaching for f/22 the next time you want more depth of field.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Stack for sharpness">
Find a scene with something close and something far that you want both sharp: rocks in the foreground, mountains behind. Shoot one frame focused at the hyperfocal distance, then a focus-bracketed run stepped from the near rock to the far ridge. Blend the run in software and compare the corners at 100 percent against the single frame. On a high-resolution file the stack will resolve detail the single frame only approximates.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="04" title="Protect the sky">
At dawn or dusk, meter a landscape with a bright sky over a dark foreground. Expose so the brightest part of the sky is just held rather than clipped, even though the foreground looks too dark on the screen. Then lift the shadows from the raw. The file will hold more than the back-of-camera preview suggested, and you will learn to trust the histogram over the picture.
</ExerciseCard>
