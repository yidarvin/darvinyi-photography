A portrait is where the whole book comes due at once. Exposure, optics, focus, light, color, and the develop module all show up in a single frame, and they all answer to one thing: a believable, flattering rendering of a person. That is what makes a portrait feel less like a skill and more like a chain. Each link is a decision you already know how to make, and the picture is only as strong as the weakest one you left to chance. This chapter walks a single portrait end to end, from the distance you stand at and the lens you reach for, through the light and the leaf-shutter fill that shape the face, to the skin tone you land in the raw file. Nothing here is new. The work is running the chain on purpose.

## Everything serves the face

A landscape can be about the light, a street frame about the moment, but a portrait is about the person, and every other decision is judged by whether it serves the face. That single rule sorts the whole process. A shallow depth of field is not a look you apply, it is a way to lift the face off its surroundings. A key light placed off to one side is not drama for its own sake, it is the shape of a cheekbone. A warm grade is not a mood, it is skin that reads as skin. When a portrait fails it is almost never because one setting was wrong in isolation. It is because two links disagreed: sharp on the ear instead of the eye, a flattering light under a green cast, a perfect exposure at a distance that ballooned the nose.

The chapters behind you each handled one link in isolation. Here they run in sequence, and the order matters because the early decisions constrain the late ones. You cannot rescue perspective in the edit, and you cannot dodge a catchlight into an eye that never had one. So the chain starts where the picture starts, at your feet.

## Distance first, then the lens

The most common portrait mistake is made before the shutter opens, by standing too close with too wide a lens. The fix begins with a fact that surprises people: the way a face is proportioned in a photograph, the apparent size of the nose against the ears, is set entirely by how far the camera stands from the subject, not by the focal length. Two frames shot from the same spot share the same perspective no matter which lens is mounted. A longer lens flatters a face only because it forces you to back away to keep the same framing, and it is the backing away that does the work.

Stand close and whatever is nearest the lens swells. On a face that is the nose and the forehead, which balloon against the ears and give the [wide-angle](/lenses-optics-and-perspective) portrait its bulbous, unflattering look. Step back and the features settle into proportion. The effect is measurable: in a controlled study that held the head framing constant across focal lengths, the facial width-to-height ratio climbed from about 1.70 at 50mm to about 1.80 at 85 to 105mm, a shift large enough that viewers rated the 105mm faces most attractive and the 50mm faces least attractive. This is why the classic portrait focal length on full frame is a short telephoto, roughly 85 through 135mm. An 85mm is the conversational length, close enough to talk to your subject at six to eight feet for a head and shoulders, far enough that the perspective flatters.

The two cameras in this book answer that decision in opposite ways, exactly as [choosing a lens](/choosing-a-lens) laid out. On the Hasselblad you reach for the portrait glass and stand back. The XCD 90V frames like a 71mm in full-frame terms and the XCD 80mm f/1.9, the fastest lens in the system, both sit in the flattering short-tele range and put you at a comfortable few paces. The Leica hands you one fixed 28mm, which is wide, so a Q3 portrait forks. Either you make an environmental portrait, letting the wide frame hold the person inside a place, which is a real and often better picture, or you step in for a tight face and accept that up close the 28mm stretches the features. What you cannot do is fake the short-tele rendering by cropping. The Q3's 75 and 90mm framelines reframe the 28mm, but they keep the deep depth of field of a 28mm, because [cropping only tightens the view](/aperture-and-depth-of-field) and never thins the plane. The compression and the separation of a real portrait lens live in the distance and the aperture, not in the crop.

## The near eye, and how thin the plane is

Once you are at the right distance with the aperture open, the depth of field on a face is startlingly thin, thinner than a head is deep. Run the standard numbers. An 85mm at f/1.8 focused two meters away holds only about 5.7 centimeters of sharpness front to back, and at f/2 and a meter and a half that falls to around 3.5. On the Hasselblad it is thinner still, because the [larger sensor](/aperture-and-depth-of-field) renders any f-number like a wider one: the 80mm f/1.9 at a meter and a half gives roughly 4.7 centimeters, and closer to two at a meter. A human head is more than twice that deep from nose to ear. You do not get to have all of it, so you choose, and the choice is always the same. Focus the near eye. The eye is the emotional center of the portrait, and when the near eye is sharp the picture reads as sharp even while the far eye, the ear, and the nose soften around it.

Landing the plane on a target a few centimeters deep, on a person who breathes and sways, is what [eye-detection autofocus](/focus-and-autofocus) is for. Both cameras do it, and both default to the eye nearest the camera, which is the one you wanted. The Q3 finds it with its hybrid system across 315 fields, the X2D II with 425 phase-detect zones, deep-learning subject tracking, and a LiDAR ranger that measures the distance outright. The old workaround, focusing with the center point and then recomposing, quietly fails at these apertures. When you lock focus and rotate the camera, the flat plane of focus swings back behind the subject by a factor of one over the cosine of the angle, and at f/1.8 that shift can exceed the entire depth of field. Worked out, an 85mm f/1.8 at five feet holds about 1.3 inches of sharpness, and a ten-degree recompose pushes the plane about 0.92 inches back, enough to move the eye out of focus entirely. Let the camera track the eye instead of swinging the frame across it.

## Put the light where it shapes the face

[Reading light](/reading-light) taught the general move, swinging one key light around a subject and watching the shadows travel. On a face those positions have names, because the nose shadow is a precise reporter of where the light stands. With the key in front and above, on the lens axis, you get butterfly lighting, a small symmetric shadow straight down under the nose, the old Paramount glamour look. Rotate the key twenty-five to fifty degrees off-axis and a little higher and the nose shadow loops down onto the cheek without joining the shadow side: loop lighting, the most versatile and natural of the set. Push past forty-five degrees and higher and the nose shadow connects with the cheek shadow, leaving a small isolated triangle of light on the shadow-side cheek, which is Rembrandt lighting. Take the key all the way to ninety degrees and it splits the face down the middle, half lit and half dark, the most dramatic pattern. The reliable starting place is the key about forty-five degrees to the side and forty-five degrees up, high enough to sculpt with a natural top-down shadow.

Which way the subject turns matters as much as where the light stands, and it has its own vocabulary. Short lighting puts the key on the side of the face turned away from the camera, so the near side falls into shadow and the face reads slimmer and more sculpted. Broad lighting does the reverse, lighting the camera-side cheek and widening the face. Short lighting is the flattering default for most people, and broad is the exception you reach for on a narrow face. One more thing decides whether an eye is alive: the catchlight, the reflection of the light source in the eye. Convention places it high, at ten or two o'clock, so the light reads as coming from slightly above and to one side. An eye without a catchlight looks dead, which is why photographers will add a light for that reflection alone.

## Fill sets the mood, and the leaf shutter sets you free

The key gives the face its shape. The fill decides how deep the shadow it casts is allowed to go, and that ratio is where the mood lives. Lighting ratio compares the lit side to the shadow side in stops, and because each stop doubles the light, the arithmetic is clean: a one-stop difference is a 2:1 ratio, two stops is 4:1, three stops is 8:1. A gentle 2:1 to 4:1 is the flattering portrait range, soft gradation with the shape still reading. Past 8:1 the shadows go black and the portrait turns moody and low-key. You set that ratio by how much you open the shadow side, whether with a reflector bouncing the key back or with a second light.

Outdoors, that second light is usually a flash, and here the daylight frame does something worth understanding: it is a double exposure of two lights, the ambient and the flash, laid on top of each other. The aperture controls both of them, but the shutter speed controls only the ambient, and that split hands you two separate moves. The first is fill. Meter the sun as your key, then add flash set roughly one to two stops below the ambient; a common starting point is flash compensation around minus 1 2/3 stops, and the flash quietly opens the shadow side and drops a catchlight in the eye without announcing itself. The second move is to overpower the sun. Drive the shutter up to darken the ambient sky, let the flash become the key that carries the face, and you get a rich, saturated background behind a cleanly lit subject at a wide aperture in broad daylight.

That second move is where your cameras have their edge, and it is the [leaf shutter](/flash-sync-and-the-leaf-advantage) paying off in a portrait. An ordinary focal-plane camera hits a wall at its flash sync speed, around 1/250, so to shoot fill at f/2 in bright sun it must switch to high-speed sync and give up two to three stops of flash power, or screw on a neutral-density filter and dim everything. The Q3 and every XCD lens sync a flash at every mechanical speed, to 1/2000 on the Q3 and 1/4000 on the newest Hasselblad glass, so you keep the wide aperture, keep full flash power, and drop the sky as far as you like. The single most useful thing a leaf shutter does for a portrait is let you balance a face against a bright background at any shutter speed you please.

## Land the skin, then leave it alone

The frame is captured. What is left is the one thing a portrait cannot get wrong, the skin, and the good news is that skin is more predictable than it looks. The skin of every person, of every ethnicity, sits in the same narrow band of hue between red and yellow, the line video engineers call the skin-tone line. Melanin changes how bright and how saturated skin is, not its fundamental hue, because that hue comes from the blood beneath it. So skin is mostly a white-balance problem, and [white balance is a correction with a right answer](/color-grading). Set the neutral, and skin falls onto its line. The classic failure is a green tint, which is what makes a face look sickly, and it is fixed on the tint axis, not by chasing the skin color directly.

When you do add color, reach for Vibrance and not Saturation, a distinction [color grading](/color-grading) flagged and portraits make urgent. Adobe's own definition is the reason: Vibrance adjusts saturation so that clipping is minimized as colors approach full saturation, moves the less saturated colors more than the already vivid ones, and, in Adobe's words, "prevents skin tones from becoming oversaturated." Saturation adjusts every color equally, and pushed on a face it turns skin into an orange mask, which is the single most common beginner edit. This is also where the book's two color sciences show their hand one last time. Hasselblad's Natural Colour Solution is tuned to keep skin smooth and minimally affected as you work, and Leica's Maestro rendering has its own warmth, so the two bodies hand you skin from slightly different starting points, and the correct-then-choose order is what brings them together.

The last temptation is to keep going, and the discipline is to stop. Real skin has pores and fine lines, and erasing them turns a face to plastic. The two honest tools both keep that texture. Dodging and burning treats a blemish as what it physically is, a small cluster of highlights and shadows, and evens those out by lightening the dark points and darkening the bright ones, which never blurs a pore. Frequency separation splits the picture into a texture layer and a color-and-tone layer so you can even out blotchy color while leaving the texture untouched. The aim, in the words of retouchers who do it for a living, is a picture where the viewer thinks "what a great portrait," not "look at the retouching," and a few remaining imperfections are exactly what keep the skin believable.

## Build the portrait

Every decision above is a link, and the widget lets you add them one at a time to a single face. It starts on a flat snapshot, deep depth and even light and a color cast, the picture a camera makes when no one is deciding. Drag the slider and the chain assembles: the aperture opens and the background falls away, the near eye takes the plane, a key light gives the face a lit and a shadow side, fill lifts the shadow to a chosen ratio and drops a catchlight, white balance clears the cast, and a final dodge and burn puts the light where you want the eye to land. Nothing added is a filter. Each notch is one earlier chapter, applied on purpose, which is the whole argument of this chapter made physical.

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Prove that distance, not the lens, shapes the face">
Photograph a willing subject's head and shoulders from about two feet away, filling the frame, then step back to eight feet and reframe to the same crop, by zooming, by swapping to a longer lens, or by cropping the file later. Compare the two. The close frame swells the nose and narrows the ears, and the far frame settles the face into proportion, even though the subject never moved. That difference is perspective, and you set it with your feet. On the Q3, this is the argument for the environmental portrait or the step back; on the X2D, for reaching for the 90V.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Feel how thin the plane is">
Open your lens to its widest aperture, frame a face at a normal portrait distance, and put the focus on the near eye with eye detection. Now look at the far eye and the ear at full magnification. At f/2 they are already softening, because the sharp zone is only a few centimeters deep. Then try focusing with the center point and recomposing, and watch the eye slip out of focus as you swing the frame. That slip is cosine error, and it is the reason eye detection exists.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="One light, four patterns">
Sit a subject by a window or a single lamp and move them, not the light, through the classic positions. Face the light for a flat front look, turn a little for loop, turn more for the Rembrandt triangle on the shadow cheek, and turn to ninety degrees for split. Watch the nose shadow report each move, and watch the catchlight ride high in the eye. Then turn the subject so the light hits the far cheek, which is short lighting, and see the face slim. You changed nothing but the angle, and you changed the whole portrait.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="04" title="Land the skin, then protect it">
Take a portrait with an obvious color cast and fix it first on white balance alone, until the skin sits neutral and the green is gone, before touching anything else. Then boost Saturation hard and watch the skin turn to orange rubber. Undo it, and do the same boost with Vibrance instead, and watch the skin hold while the rest of the frame lifts. That is the difference the portrait photographer lives on, and it is why Vibrance is the slider you reach for on a face.
</ExerciseCard>
