A [portrait](/portrait-in-practice) is a chain you control, run at your own pace. A street photograph is the opposite. The picture assembles itself in front of you, holds for a fraction of a second, and you either had the camera ready or you did not. There is no second take, no arranging the light, no asking the subject to hold still. So the craft of street and documentary work is not reaction, which is always a beat too slow, but readiness. You settle everything you can in advance, the exposure, the focus, the frame, so that when the moment arrives the only decision left is when to press. This chapter is about building that readiness, and about the two cameras' different places in it. The Leica Q3 becomes a reflex, an extension of the eye. The Hasselblad X2D is built for the slower, returning work of a documentary project.

## The moment you cannot stage

The idea that organizes everything here is Henri Cartier-Bresson's, and it is worth stating precisely because it is so often stated wrong. His 1952 book was called _Images à la sauvette_, which means something like images taken on the run. The phrase everyone remembers, "the decisive moment," was the American publisher's title, lifted from a line by the seventeenth-century Cardinal de Retz that Cartier-Bresson used as his epigraph: there is nothing in this world that does not have a decisive moment. What he meant by it was specific. Photography, he wrote, is "the simultaneous recognition, in a fraction of a second, of the significance of an event" together with the precise organization of forms that gives the event its shape. Two things at once, and the second matters as much as the first: the meaning of what is happening, and the geometry of how it sits in the frame.

The common misreading is that the decisive moment means the peak of the action, the highest jump, the hardest laugh. Cartier-Bresson's own foundation calls that an error. The decisive instant is the one where the forms lock into order, when the walking figure, the shadow, and the doorway line up and the frame composes itself. It is a compositional peak, not a dramatic one, and it is why he refused to crop. He printed the full negative with a black border from the film rim to prove nothing was trimmed, because if the geometry was not already right in the viewfinder, at the instant of pressing, it could not be rescued later. The moment and the frame are decided together, or not at all.

Recognizing that instant is a matter of anticipation, not reflex. By the time you have consciously seen the alignment and told your finger to move, it is gone. The photographers who catch it are reading the scene a moment ahead, pressing on the expectation that the forms are about to meet. That is a skill you build by shooting, but it only has room to work if nothing else is competing for your attention. Which is the whole practical argument of this chapter. Every setting you can decide in advance is attention freed for the one thing you cannot.

## Prefocus the street, then forget the focus

Autofocus, even the fast [eye and subject detection](/focus-and-autofocus) that makes a portrait easy, is the wrong tool for a fleeting candid. It has to find the subject, confirm, and fire, and those tenths of a second are exactly the ones the moment lives in. It also announces itself, hunting visibly as you raise the camera. The old answer, still the fastest, is to take focus out of the moment entirely. You prefocus once, to a fixed distance, and let [depth of field](/aperture-and-depth-of-field) hold a whole zone of the street acceptably sharp. Anyone who walks into that zone is already in focus. This is zone focusing, and Ricoh built a whole street camera around a version of it called snap focus.

The distance you set is the hyperfocal distance, the focus point that stretches the far edge of sharpness all the way to infinity while keeping the nearest possible foreground. Focus there and everything from roughly half that distance to infinity is sharp. The formula is H = f² / (N·c) + f, where f is the focal length, N the aperture, and c the circle of confusion, the largest blur spot that still reads as a point, about 0.03mm on full frame. The numbers for a 28mm lens are friendly. At f/8 the hyperfocal distance is about 3.3 meters, so prefocus there and the street is sharp from about 1.6 meters to infinity. Stop down to f/11 and the near edge moves in to about 1.2 meters. Open up to f/5.6 and it falls back to about 2.35 meters. Set the focus once, and for the rest of the block you never touch it again.

The cost of the zone is light. A deep zone needs a small aperture, and f/8 in the shade or after dark means a slow shutter or a high ISO. The working method is to set the aperture you want for the zone, let [auto ISO](/metering-and-exposure-decisions) float, and hold a minimum shutter speed fast enough to freeze a walking stride, around 1/250. In daylight this is effortless. At night the zone gets expensive, the ISO climbs, and many photographers give up the technique and shoot wide open on autofocus instead, accepting a thinner plane. The maxim that captures the whole trade is "f/8 and be there," usually credited to Weegee, probably wrongly, and better documented as old advice from _National Geographic_. Whoever said it, it is half the job. The f/8 is the zone. Being there is everything else.

## One wide lens, and standing close

Street photography leans wide, and the reason is not the look of a wide lens but where it makes you stand. A 28mm lens on full frame sees about 75 degrees across the diagonal, wide enough to hold a person and the place around them in one frame. To fill that frame with a subject you have to be close, a few feet away, inside the scene rather than watching it from across the road. That closeness is the point. Robert Capa's line, "if your pictures aren't good enough, you're not close enough," is really an argument for the wide lens: it drags you into the event, and the picture carries the intimacy of having been there. A long lens does the opposite, holding you back and putting a coolness between you and the subject.

A wide lens does not distort perspective on its own. As [lenses and perspective](/lenses-optics-and-perspective) laid out, the swelling of what is near and the shrinking of what is far come from being close, not from the focal length, and the wide lens simply obliges you to be close. So the discipline it demands is to fill the frame. Get a strong foreground within arm's reach, or the wide angle just renders everything small and distant and the frame reads as empty. Garry Winogrand, who shot most of his career on two Leica M4 bodies with 28mm lenses, worked exactly this way, standing in the churn of a sidewalk at arm's length from his subjects and tilting the camera to pack the corners. He chose the 28mm, he said, because it sat closest to his angle of attention.

The one fixed [28mm](/choosing-a-lens) on the Leica Q3 turns this constraint into a habit. With a single focal length you stop deciding what lens to use and start seeing in one frame. You know, before the camera is at your eye, what 28mm will take in from where you stand, so the only compositional choice left is your feet: where to stand, and how close to get. That is the same economy the zone gives you with focus. One less variable in the moment, one more piece of attention for the picture.

## The camera as a reflex

Put the pieces together and the Q3 stops feeling like a camera you operate and starts feeling like a reflex. The exposure is pre-set: aperture priority at f/8, auto ISO floating underneath, a minimum shutter around 1/250. The focus is pre-set: the lens has a real mechanical ring with a depth-of-field scale, so you can dial the zone by hand and read the sharp range straight off the barrel, and a twist of the macro ring drops the close focus to 17cm when you want a detail. The frame is pre-set: one 28mm angle you already see in. And the camera stays quiet. Its leaf shutter is soft to begin with, and in electronic-shutter mode it is silent, so nothing about raising it announces that a picture is being made. It is small for a full-frame camera, about 743 grams, with no protruding zoom or swapped lenses to mark you as serious. All of that adds up to a camera you can carry all day and lift without thinking.

The 60-megapixel sensor buys one more reflex. The Q3 shows crop framelines for 35, 50, 75, and 90mm views inside the 28mm frame while keeping the full scene visible around them, and the raw file is always the uncropped 28mm capture, so you can commit to a tighter framing in the moment and still change your mind later. The catch, worth saying plainly, is that every crop is still a crop of one 28mm lens. It keeps the 28mm depth of field and the 28mm perspective. As [aperture and depth of field](/aperture-and-depth-of-field) showed, cropping narrows the view but never thins the plane, so a 90mm frameline gives you the reach of a short telephoto with none of its background separation. It reframes the story. It does not compress it.

The Hasselblad X2D II sits at the other end of this. It is not the camera you raise without thinking. It is larger, slower to work, and its 100-megapixel files ask to be considered. But that is precisely what suits it to the documentary side of this chapter, the sustained project where you return to a place or a person over weeks and want every frame to hold up large. Its [LiDAR-assisted autofocus](/focus-and-autofocus) is good enough that candor is still possible when you want it. The division is clean. The Q3 is the reflex you carry for the street's chance encounters. The X2D is the deliberate instrument you bring to a story you have chosen.

## Fishing, not hunting

With the camera ready, the work becomes seeing, and the most reliable way to see a good frame is to stop chasing it. The tactic street photographers call fishing is to find a stage first, a patch of good [light](/reading-light), a strong graphic background, a doorway or a shaft of sun, and then wait for a subject to walk into it. Joel Meyerowitz has described standing at a spot with the right light and simply waiting until the right person arrived to complete it. You are not hunting moving subjects, you are pre-composing an empty frame and letting the moment come to you. It is patient, it looks like loitering, and it produces pictures that a faster, more frantic approach walks right past.

The frames that reward the longest looking are usually layered, built with something happening in the foreground, the middle, and the background at once, held together by [composition](/advanced-composition) so the eye can read all of it. Alex Webb is the reference, stacking several simultaneous events and zones of light into one frame that stays legible. Layering is hard because it asks the whole depth of the frame to cooperate at the same instant, which is another reason the deep zone matters: it keeps all those planes sharp so any of them can carry the picture.

And then there is the part no one likes, which is that the keeper rate on the street is brutally low. Working a scene means shooting it many times, from several positions, to extract one frame that holds. Magnum's own contact sheets show it, a roll of near-misses around a single picture that worked. Winogrand is the cautionary tale here: he died leaving thousands of rolls undeveloped, hundreds of thousands of frames he never even looked at, seeing pushed so hard that editing never happened. The discipline is the opposite. Shoot freely, then cut without mercy. The edit is where the work is finished.

## Street, documentary, and the right to the picture

Street and documentary overlap, but they are not the same thing, and the difference is intent. Street photography is candid and driven by chance, an unrepeatable arrangement of strangers and light in public space, often as much about form as about subject. Documentary is a sustained project with a purpose, the Tate's "straightforward and accurate representation of people, places, objects and events," made to bear witness and often to argue for change. Robert Frank's _The Americans_ is the model: a Guggenheim grant, two years on the road, tens of thousands of frames pared to 83, an outsider's cold-eyed record of a country. Dorothea Lange and the Farm Security Administration made the case earlier, using the camera as an instrument of policy, introducing America to Americans in the Depression. The tradition is wide enough to hold Vivian Maier composing into a waist-level Rolleiflex so no one saw her look, Daido Moriyama's deliberately grainy, blurred, out-of-focus frames, and Bruce Gilden's close flash, fired inches from a face, on the principle that "if you can smell the street" in the picture, it is a street photograph.

That range raises a real question of what you are allowed to do, and the answer splits into law and ethics. In the United States, photographing anything in plain view from a public place is a constitutional right, and there is no general need for consent to take the picture; the ACLU is explicit about it. The legal line is not the taking but the use. Editorial, news, and artistic work is protected, and courts have upheld even the sale and exhibition of a candid street portrait of an unaware subject as free expression. Commercial use, putting a recognizable person in an advertisement, needs a signed model release. The law also changes hard at the border. France's droit à l'image gives a person control over their own image whether the photo was made in public or not, so publishing an identifiable face generally requires consent. Germany restricts publishing rather than shooting, with exceptions for public figures and for people who appear only incidentally.

<Callout>
The law is the floor, not the standard. Where you photograph decides what is legal, and it varies enough that a frame you can freely publish in New York may need written consent in Paris. But legality does not settle whether you should press the shutter. The documentary tradition carries its own duties, written into the codes of the NPPA and Magnum: do not stage or alter the event you claim to be recording, minimize the harm your picture can do, and take special care with children and vulnerable people. The working rule most photographers land on is to photograph people with the dignity you would want, and to aim the camera up more often than down.
</Callout>

## Catch the moment

Everything above is in service of the one variable you cannot pre-decide. The widget locks the rest, the exposure, the zone focus, the 28mm frame, exactly as your camera would be, and leaves you only the moment. A figure crosses a gap between two pillars. Scrub time and hunt for the instant the picture composes itself, the walker centered in the frame on an open stride, and press the shutter there. Watch the strip under the slider as you go. The window where the frame works is a spike, not a plateau, which is the whole reason the decisive moment has to be anticipated rather than caught by reaction. By the time you see it, you are already late. You press for where the moment is about to be.

Street and documentary work is the discipline of readiness. Because the picture assembles itself and holds for only a fraction of a second, you decide everything you can before it arrives. Prefocus to the hyperfocal distance and a whole zone of the street stays sharp, so a 28mm at f/8 holds from about 1.6 meters to infinity with no refocusing. Do all of that and the moment is the single thing left to your attention, which is exactly where Cartier-Bresson put it: the instant the significance of an event and the geometry of the frame lock together. The Q3 makes the whole apparatus a reflex; the X2D is the slower instrument for the documentary project. And the moment, always, is anticipated rather than caught. ## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Set the zone and never touch it">
Put the camera in manual focus, dial the distance to about 3 meters, and stop down to f/8. Now walk a block and photograph whatever happens without ever refocusing. Do not raise the camera to check, just frame and press. Afterward, look at how much of each frame is sharp. Almost everything from a couple of steps away to the far background will be, and you will feel how much faster you shot with focus taken out of the moment.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Fish one spot">
Find a stage and wait at it. Pick a patch of good light, a strong background, or a doorway that frames well, then stand there and do not move for ten minutes. Watch people enter the empty frame you have already composed and press when one of them completes it. This is the opposite of chasing subjects, and it will teach you more about anticipation than a mile of walking.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="One step closer">
Take a street frame from where you would normally stand, then force yourself two steps closer and take it again. On the fixed 28mm the second frame will feel almost uncomfortably near, and it will almost always be the better picture: the foreground fills, the viewer is inside the scene, and the wide lens finally has something to hold. Capa's line is a physical instruction. Follow it literally once and feel the difference.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="04" title="Cut to one">
From a whole outing, choose exactly one frame to keep. Not five, not a favorites folder, one. Sit with the near-misses and notice what separates the picture that works from the four that almost did: usually the moment landed a half-second more precisely, or the geometry sat a little cleaner. The edit is where you learn to see, and the ruthlessness is the lesson.
</ExerciseCard>
